\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Forecasting how single cells move between states---across donors, batches, timepoints and
perturbations---is a core single-cell analysis task, yet it is confounded by biological and
technical nuisance variation and by the rarity of the cell states that matter most. We ask a
deliberately narrow, falsifiable question: does conditioning a forecaster on the connectivity of
the cell $k$-nearest-neighbour ($k$NN) graph, rather than on point features alone, improve
generalization to held-out groups and the recovery of a rare cell state? We build a fully seeded,
fully controlled \emph{synthetic} branching lineage with donor, batch, perturbation and pseudotime
metadata and a deliberately rare subpopulation ($\sim$\SI{4}{\percent} of cells), and evaluate two
method pairs under four leakage-checked split protocols (donor, batch, time and perturbation
transfer). Graph-smoothed label propagation on the cell $k$NN graph improves held-out forecast
accuracy over a plain $k$NN classifier on all four splits---by \num{0.0454}
(donor: \num{0.7004} vs \num{0.6550}), \num{0.0640} (batch: \num{0.6995} vs \num{0.6355}),
\num{0.0900} (time: \num{0.5474} vs \num{0.4574}) and \num{0.0385} (perturbation: \num{0.6875} vs
\num{0.6490}); the mean gain across splits is \num{0.0595}. We caution up front that this
headline is \emph{not} a clean measurement of graph diffusion: the label-propagation forecaster
is \emph{transductive} (it builds the $k$NN graph over all cells and so sees the held-out cells'
connectivity at inference), whereas the plain $k$NN baseline is \emph{inductive} (fit on training
cells only). The accuracy gap is therefore confounded by this transductive-versus-inductive
asymmetry rather than attributable to diffusion alone; a fair inductive control (e.g.\ label
spreading restricted to the training graph) is not yet run in the accompanying repository, and we
flag the \num{0.0595} figure as an upper bound to be read with this caveat. The same diffusion recovers a rare
state that the baseline almost never finds (rare-state recall up to \num{0.4618} on the batch split
versus near-zero), and a topology-aware active-sampling policy (uncertainty $\times$ inverse local
density) raises rare-state recall to \num{0.3556} versus \num{0.0247} for random querying. These
gains carry a small, honestly reported calibration cost: expected calibration error is slightly
\emph{worse} for the graph-smoothed forecaster on every split. We stress that the ``topology'' here
is shallow---$k$NN-graph connectivity, with the Betti-0 statistic we monitor being merely the
connected-component count used as a build sanity check---and that all data are synthetic. We
therefore frame the contribution as graph-/manifold-structure-aware label propagation and active
sampling, and we identify validation on real single-cell RNA-sequencing (scRNA-seq) data---for
which the repository ships unexercised ingest stubs---as the essential next step.
\end{abstract}

Single-cell measurements resolve continuous trajectories of differentiation, activation and
treatment response, and a recurring analysis goal is to \emph{forecast} the state a cell or
population will occupy under conditions not directly observed: a donor not yet profiled, a
technical batch run later, a later point along a developmental trajectory, or an untested
perturbation. The data that support such forecasts share two structural features. First, cells do
not occupy feature space uniformly; they concentrate near a low-dimensional manifold that is
routinely approximated by a $k$-nearest-neighbour ($k$NN) graph before clustering, embedding or
pseudotime inference \cite{wolf2018scanpy,mcinnes2018umap,becht2019umap,vandermaaten2008tsne,belkin2003laplacian,luxburg2007spectral}. Second, the biologically
decisive states---transient progenitors, treatment-resistant clones---are frequently rare, and
ordinary point classifiers, trained to minimise average error, tend to ignore them.

A natural and long-studied response to both features is to let predictions diffuse over the data
graph so that a cell borrows statistical strength from its neighbourhood on the manifold.
Graph-based semi-supervised learning by label propagation \cite{zhu2002labelprop,zhou2004local} is
the canonical instance, and message-passing on cell graphs underlies much of modern single-cell
trajectory analysis \cite{haghverdi2016diffusion,trapnell2014monocle,lamanno2018velocity,coifman2006diffusion}.
A complementary lever is to choose \emph{which} cells to annotate. Active learning
\cite{settles2009active,lewis1994sequential} prioritises informative queries, and when
informativeness is combined with a manifold-density signal it can be steered toward the sparse
regions where rare states live.

This final manuscript in the ordered series transfers the same graph-conditioned,
uncertainty-aware and resource-accounted style from quantum applications to a
biomedical graph problem.  Earlier papers in the sequence develop
graph-conditioned QAOA, topology-conditioned QAOA, commutator-graph Hamiltonian
simulation, learned Trotter residuals, CPTP-constrained open-system
identification, drift monitoring, selective QEC decoding, covariance-aware
hybrid surface-code decoding and gauge-equivariant syndrome-bundle
decoding~\cite{huynh2026uqqaoa,huynh2026topoqaoa,huynh2026topoham,huynh2026liergtc,huynh2026cptppinn,huynh2026drift,huynh2026selectiveqec,huynh2026covdec,huynh2026lagnos}.
The Hamiltonian-simulation papers of Nguyen and Jing supply the required
fixed-depth and representation-theoretic quantum context for the
series~\cite{nguyen2025zassenhaus,jing2026complexity}.  The present manuscript does
not make a quantum-hardware claim; it carries the graph and active-query design
principles into a synthetic single-cell forecasting benchmark.

We do not claim a new algorithm. Instead we isolate, under controlled and leakage-checked
conditions, the value of \emph{graph structure} for single-cell forecasting by holding the
neighbourhood information fixed and toggling only whether the model diffuses over the graph.
Concretely: (i) we compare graph-smoothed label propagation against a plain $k$NN classifier that
sees the same neighbourhood radius but performs no diffusion. We are explicit that this comparison
does \emph{not} cleanly isolate diffusion: the propagation forecaster is transductive (it builds the
graph over all cells, including the held-out set) while the $k$NN baseline is inductive (training
cells only), so the measured gap mixes the diffusion step with a transductive information advantage
and overstates the value of diffusion per se (Sec.~\ref{sec:confound}); and (ii) we compare a
topology-aware active-sampling policy---uncertainty times inverse local density---against uniform
random querying for rare-state recall. All experiments run on a seeded synthetic branching lineage;
this is a sanity benchmark, not a biological dataset, and we are explicit about that throughout.
Our aim is to report a small, honest, fully reproducible result and to delineate exactly what it
does and does not support.

\subsection{A controlled synthetic lineage with replicate, condition and pseudotime structure}
Our benchmark is a seeded synthetic branching lineage that reproduces the replicate, condition and
pseudotime structure a real forecaster must handle, while remaining bit-for-bit reproducible
(Figs.~\ref{fig:schematic},~\ref{fig:lineage}). Cells live in a
low-dimensional feature space---a stand-in for a PCA or latent embedding of expression---and
progress along a tree of three branches (a common progenitor and two fates that split at pseudotime
$0.45$) as Gaussian blobs whose mean drifts along each branch. Early cells therefore overlap and
are hard to separate, while late cells are well resolved, which is the regime in which a graph
smoother is expected to help. Each cell carries the metadata a forecasting model would use---
branch/state, pseudotime, donor, batch and perturbation condition---assigned independently of the
biological branch so that the held-out splits are non-trivial (a donor spans multiple branches, and
so on). One state is carved out of the late tip of the second fate to be deliberately \emph{rare},
comprising \SI{4}{\percent} of cells in the reported configuration. The full-scale configuration
generates \num{3000} cells with \num{16} features, \num{8} donors, \num{4} batches and \num{5}
perturbation conditions, and is run over \num{5} random seeds; the run completed in
\SI{20.8}{\second} with a peak memory of \SI{320.6}{\mega\byte}. Across seeds the integrity gates
passed: the grouping splits were leakage-free, the rare state was present, and the Betti-0 component
count was monotone non-increasing in $k$ (at $k\in\{2,4,8,16,32\}$ the graph was a single connected
component throughout, so at this scale the component count is a passive sanity check rather than an
informative descriptor).

\subsection{Graph diffusion gives consistent but modest accuracy gains on held-out transfer}
Under all four leakage-checked split protocols, graph-smoothed label propagation forecasts held-out
cell states more accurately than the plain $k$NN baseline that receives the same annotated cells and
the same neighbourhood radius (Table~\ref{tab:main}, Fig.~\ref{fig:forecast}a). The improvement is
consistent but modest. On the donor split, accuracy rises from \num{0.6550} to \num{0.7004}
($+\num{0.0454}$); on the batch split from \num{0.6355} to \num{0.6995} ($+\num{0.0640}$); on the
time split---the hardest protocol, because it extrapolates forward along pseudotime---from
\num{0.4574} to \num{0.5474} ($+\num{0.0900}$); and on the perturbation split from \num{0.6490} to
\num{0.6875} ($+\num{0.0385}$). Averaged over the four splits, the graph-smoothed forecaster reaches
\num{0.6587} accuracy against \num{0.5992} for the baseline, a mean gain of \num{0.0595}. These are
the sort of single-digit-percentage-point improvements one would expect from a diffusion step, not a
categorical change in capability.

\paragraph{A transductive-versus-inductive confound inflates the headline.}
\label{sec:confound}
This accuracy gain must be read with one structural caveat that we did not control for. The
label-propagation forecaster builds the $k$NN graph over \emph{all} cells and diffuses the training
labels across it (\texttt{policy.py}, \texttt{LabelPropagationForecaster}); it therefore observes the
held-out cells' features and graph connectivity at inference time and is \emph{transductive}. The
$k$NN baseline, by contrast, is fit on the labelled training cells only and is \emph{inductive}. The
two methods thus differ in \emph{two} ways at once---diffusion versus no diffusion, \emph{and}
transductive versus inductive access to the test set---so the \num{0.0595} mean gap is not a clean
estimate of the value of diffusion. The honest reading is that a substantial share of the headline is
plausibly this information asymmetry rather than diffusion, and that the diffusion-specific effect
measured against a matched \emph{inductive} graph control (for example label spreading restricted to
the training graph, or out-of-sample extension of the propagated field) would be materially smaller.
We do not report such a control because it is not yet implemented in the accompanying repository; we
flag this as the primary methodological gap and treat \num{0.0595} as an upper bound on the
diffusion-attributable gain. The rare-state-recall and active-sampling effects below are reported
under the same transductive setting and inherit the same caveat.

\subsection{Diffusion and active sampling recover a rare state the baseline misses}
The clearer effect is on the rare state (Fig.~\ref{fig:forecast}b,c). The plain $k$NN baseline
essentially never recovers the rare population: its rare-state recall is \num{0.0000} (donor),
\num{0.0320} (batch), \num{0.0000} (time) and \num{0.0286} (perturbation). Graph-smoothed
propagation improves rare-state recall to \num{0.1520} (donor), \num{0.4618} (batch) and
\num{0.3393} (perturbation). On the time split, recall remains \num{0.0000} for \emph{both} methods:
extrapolating forward in pseudotime places the rare late-tip cells entirely beyond the training
distribution, and diffusion over the graph cannot manufacture a label class that was never reachable
from the annotated set. We report this null result as-is.

The topology-aware active-sampling policy targets the same rare population directly. Starting from a
small random label seed and spending a fixed query budget, scoring unlabeled cells by forecast
uncertainty times inverse local density raises rare-state recall on the remaining unlabeled cells to
\num{0.3556} (95\% CI $\pm\num{0.1597}$), versus \num{0.0247} ($\pm\num{0.0285}$) for uniform random
querying---a lift of \num{0.3309}. The mechanism is visible in the query composition: the topology
policy spends \SI{36.7}{\percent} of its queries on genuinely rare cells, against \SI{3.0}{\percent}
for random sampling, which is close to the \SI{4}{\percent} base rate. The wide confidence intervals
on the per-split rare-recall numbers (for example $\pm\num{0.3125}$ on the batch split) reflect the
small absolute count of rare cells per held-out group and should temper any strong reading of the
point estimates.

\subsection{The accuracy and recall gains carry a small calibration cost}
The graph-smoothed forecaster is slightly \emph{worse} calibrated than the baseline on every split.
Expected calibration error (ECE) is \num{0.1247} versus \num{0.1073} (donor), \num{0.1383} versus
\num{0.0905} (batch), \num{0.1328} versus \num{0.0970} (time) and \num{0.1649} versus \num{0.1184}
(perturbation). Diffusion sharpens the predicted class probabilities and, in this setting, makes
them modestly overconfident relative to empirical accuracy. We report ECE alongside accuracy and
recall in Table~\ref{tab:main} precisely so that this trade-off is not hidden: the method buys
accuracy and rare-state recall at the price of a small, consistent calibration penalty, and an
application that depends on calibrated probabilities would need post-hoc recalibration
\cite{guo2017calibration}.

\subsection{Theory of graph-structure-aware forecasting}
\label{sec:theory}
We now make precise, and prove in full, every structural property that the
forecaster of Figs.~\ref{fig:schematic}--\ref{fig:forecast} relies upon. The
development is self-contained: we fix notation (Definition~\ref{def:graph}),
establish the Dirichlet-energy identity and the spectral facts it implies
(Lemma~\ref{lem:dirichlet}, Corollary~\ref{cor:nullspace}), prove that the
connectivity diagnostic we monitor is monotone in the neighbourhood size
(Proposition~\ref{prop:betti}), characterise label propagation as the unique
minimiser of a convex graph-regularised objective together with its exact
non-identifiability limitation (Theorem~\ref{thm:label-prop},
Corollary~\ref{cor:nonident}), and finally show that the normalised power
iteration the code actually runs converges geometrically to a closed-form fixed
point that is itself the minimiser of a normalised energy
(Theorem~\ref{thm:spectrum}--Theorem~\ref{thm:fixedpoint}). Each result is tied
to the experiment it underwrites. Throughout, $\mathbf{1}$ denotes the all-ones
vector, $\|\cdot\|_2$ the Euclidean norm, $\|\cdot\|_F$ the Frobenius norm,
$\operatorname{Tr}$ the trace, and for a matrix $X$ we write $X\succeq0$
($X\succ0$) for positive semidefinite (positive definite).

\begin{definition}[Cell graph, degree, Laplacian]
\label{def:graph}
Let $A\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times n}$ be the symmetric adjacency of the $k$NN cell
graph on $n$ cells: $A=A^\top$, $A_{ij}\ge0$, $A_{ii}=0$, and $A_{ij}>0$ iff
cells $i,j$ are joined by an undirected edge. The \emph{degree} of cell $i$ is
$d_i=\sum_{j}A_{ij}\ge0$, collected in $D=\operatorname{diag}(d_1,\dots,d_n)$.
The \emph{combinatorial graph Laplacian} is $L=D-A$. The \emph{Betti-0 number}
$\beta_0(A)$ is the number of connected components of the graph, i.e.\ the number
of equivalence classes of the reachability relation on $\{1,\dots,n\}$ induced
by the edges of $A$.
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}[Dirichlet-energy identity and positive semidefiniteness]
\label{lem:dirichlet}
For every $f\in\mathbb{R}^{n}$,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:dirichlet}
f^\top L f \;=\; \tfrac12\sum_{i=1}^{n}\sum_{j=1}^{n} A_{ij}\,(f_i-f_j)^2 \;\ge\; 0,
\end{equation}
so $L\succeq0$. More generally, for $F\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times c}$,
$\operatorname{Tr}(F^\top L F)=\tfrac12\sum_{i,j}A_{ij}\|F_i-F_j\|_2^2$, where
$F_i$ is the $i$-th row of $F$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Expand the quadratic form using $L=D-A$:
\[
f^\top L f = \sum_i d_i f_i^2 - \sum_{i,j} A_{ij} f_i f_j
= \sum_{i}\Big(\sum_j A_{ij}\Big) f_i^2 - \sum_{i,j} A_{ij} f_i f_j,
\]
where the first equality uses $d_i=\sum_j A_{ij}$. Now use the symmetry
$A_{ij}=A_{ji}$ to write the diagonal term symmetrically,
$\sum_i d_i f_i^2 = \tfrac12\sum_{i,j}A_{ij}(f_i^2+f_j^2)$, since
$\sum_{i,j}A_{ij}f_i^2=\sum_i d_i f_i^2$ and likewise
$\sum_{i,j}A_{ij}f_j^2=\sum_j d_j f_j^2=\sum_i d_i f_i^2$. Therefore
\[
f^\top L f = \tfrac12\sum_{i,j}A_{ij}\big(f_i^2 + f_j^2 - 2f_i f_j\big)
= \tfrac12\sum_{i,j}A_{ij}(f_i-f_j)^2.
\]
Because $A_{ij}\ge0$ and $(f_i-f_j)^2\ge0$, every summand is nonnegative, so
$f^\top L f\ge0$ for all $f$, i.e.\ $L\succeq0$. The matrix statement follows by
applying \eqref{eq:dirichlet} columnwise: writing $F=[f^{(1)}\cdots f^{(c)}]$,
$\operatorname{Tr}(F^\top L F)=\sum_{\ell=1}^{c}(f^{(\ell)})^\top L f^{(\ell)}
=\tfrac12\sum_{i,j}A_{ij}\sum_{\ell}(F_{i\ell}-F_{j\ell})^2
=\tfrac12\sum_{i,j}A_{ij}\|F_i-F_j\|_2^2$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Null space of $L$ and the spectral count of components]
\label{cor:nullspace}
$L\mathbf{1}=0$, so constant label vectors cost zero Dirichlet energy. Moreover
the multiplicity of the eigenvalue $0$ of $L$ equals $\beta_0(A)$, the number of
connected components. In particular the smallest eigenvalue of $L$ is $0$, and
$L\succ0$ on the orthogonal complement of its null space.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
$ (L\mathbf{1})_i=d_i-\sum_j A_{ij}=0$ by definition of $d_i$, so
$\mathbf{1}\in\ker L$. For the multiplicity claim, suppose $f\in\ker L$. By
\eqref{eq:dirichlet}, $f^\top L f=0$ forces $A_{ij}(f_i-f_j)^2=0$ for every
$i,j$; hence $f_i=f_j$ whenever $A_{ij}>0$, i.e.\ $f$ is constant along every
edge and therefore constant on each connected component. Conversely, any vector
that is constant on each component lies in $\ker L$ by the same identity (all
cross-edge differences vanish). Thus $\ker L$ is exactly the space of
component-wise constant vectors, which has dimension equal to the number of
components $\beta_0(A)$; choosing the indicator vector of each component gives a
basis. Since $L\succeq0$ (Lemma~\ref{lem:dirichlet}) and is symmetric, all
eigenvalues are real and nonnegative and the geometric multiplicity of the
eigenvalue $0$ equals $\dim\ker L=\beta_0(A)$; on $(\ker L)^\perp$ the form is
strictly positive, so $L\succ0$ there.
\end{proof}

Corollary~\ref{cor:nullspace} is the formal content of the ``Betti-0 ties to the
zero-eigenvalue multiplicity'' statement and underlies why a label-free component
cannot be resolved (Corollary~\ref{cor:nonident}).

\begin{proposition}[Monotonicity of the connectivity diagnostic]
\label{prop:betti}
Let $A^{(k)}$ denote the symmetric $k$NN adjacency built with neighbourhood size
$k$ (either-direction symmetrisation: an edge $\{i,j\}$ is present iff $j$ is
among the $k$ nearest neighbours of $i$ or $i$ is among the $k$ nearest
neighbours of $j$). Then the edge set is monotone non-decreasing in $k$,
$E(A^{(k)})\subseteq E(A^{(k+1)})$, and consequently the Betti-0 number is
monotone non-increasing: $\beta_0\!\big(A^{(k+1)}\big)\le \beta_0\!\big(A^{(k)}\big)$
for all $k\ge1$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Fix a cell $i$ and let $N_k(i)$ be its set of $k$ nearest neighbours (self
excluded), ordered by increasing distance with ties broken by a fixed rule. By
construction $N_k(i)\subseteq N_{k+1}(i)$: enlarging the budget from $k$ to $k+1$
only appends the next-nearest cell and removes none. Hence the directed edge set
$\{(i,j):j\in N_k(i)\}$ grows with $k$. Symmetrisation is monotone in the
directed edge set (it takes the entrywise maximum of the directed adjacency and
its transpose), so $E(A^{(k)})\subseteq E(A^{(k+1)})$. Adding edges to a graph
can only merge connected components, never split them: if $i,j$ are reachable in
$A^{(k)}$ they remain reachable in $A^{(k+1)}$, so each component of $A^{(k)}$ is
contained in a component of $A^{(k+1)}$, giving a surjection from components of
$A^{(k)}$ onto components of $A^{(k+1)}$. A surjection cannot increase
cardinality, whence $\beta_0(A^{(k+1)})\le\beta_0(A^{(k)})$.
\end{proof}

Proposition~\ref{prop:betti} is the property asserted by the build-time
monotonicity gate over the neighbourhood sizes $k\in\{2,4,8,16,32\}$; at the
reported scale the curve is the constant $\beta_0=1$ (a single component), so the
diagnostic passes vacuously and is, as we stress throughout, a passive sanity
check rather than an informative descriptor (Fig.~\ref{fig:schematic}).

\paragraph{Label propagation as a variational estimator.}
The graph-smoothed forecaster used in Fig.~\ref{fig:forecast} is label
propagation on the cell $k$NN graph. The following theorem states exactly what it
optimises, proves the minimiser is unique on every component that carries a
label, and---kept deliberately conditional---identifies the precise situation in
which uniqueness fails.

\begin{theorem}[Unique graph-regularised extension of labels]
\label{thm:label-prop}
Let $A=A^\top\succeq0$ be the $k$NN affinity, $L=D-A$ the Laplacian, $Y\in
\mathbb{R}^{n\times c}$ the one-hot label matrix (rows of unlabelled cells
arbitrary), and $M=\operatorname{diag}(m_1,\dots,m_n)$ with $m_i=1$ if cell $i$ is
labelled and $m_i=0$ otherwise. For any fidelity weight $\mu>0$ the objective
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:objective}
J(F)=\operatorname{Tr}(F^\top L F)+\mu\,\|M(F-Y)\|_F^2
\end{equation}
is convex in $F$, and any stationary point satisfies the normal equations
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:normal}
(L+\mu M)\,F=\mu M Y.
\end{equation}
If \emph{every} connected component of the graph contains at least one labelled
cell, then $L+\mu M\succ0$, the minimiser $F^\star=\mu(L+\mu M)^{-1}MY$ is unique,
and it is the minimum-Dirichlet-energy extension of the observed labels subject
to the fidelity penalty $\mu$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
\emph{Convexity.} By Lemma~\ref{lem:dirichlet}, $F\mapsto\operatorname{Tr}(F^\top
L F)$ is a nonnegative quadratic form (Hessian $2L\succeq0$ in each column), hence
convex; and $F\mapsto\|M(F-Y)\|_F^2$ is convex as the squared norm of an affine
map. A sum of convex functions is convex, so $J$ is convex.

\emph{Stationarity.} Write $J(F)=\operatorname{Tr}(F^\top L F)+\mu
\operatorname{Tr}\!\big((F-Y)^\top M^\top M (F-Y)\big)$ and use $M^\top M=M$
(since $M$ is a $0/1$ diagonal idempotent, $M^2=M$). The Fr\'echet derivative is
\[
\nabla_F J(F)=2LF+2\mu M(F-Y).
\]
Indeed, perturbing $F\to F+\epsilon H$ and collecting the first-order term,
$\frac{d}{d\epsilon}\big|_{0}\operatorname{Tr}\big((F+\epsilon H)^\top L
(F+\epsilon H)\big)=2\operatorname{Tr}(H^\top L F)$ using $L=L^\top$, and
$\frac{d}{d\epsilon}\big|_{0}\mu\|M(F+\epsilon H-Y)\|_F^2
=2\mu\operatorname{Tr}\big(H^\top M(F-Y)\big)$. Setting $\nabla_F J=0$ gives
$2LF+2\mu M(F-Y)=0$, i.e.\ \eqref{eq:normal}.

\emph{Definiteness under the labelled-component hypothesis.} The matrix
$G:=L+\mu M$ is symmetric, and for any $v\in\mathbb{R}^n$,
\[
v^\top G v = \underbrace{v^\top L v}_{\ge0}+\mu\underbrace{v^\top M v}_{=\mu\sum_{i:\,m_i=1}v_i^2\ \ge0}\ \ge0,
\]
so $G\succeq0$. Suppose $v^\top G v=0$. Then both nonnegative terms vanish:
$v^\top L v=0$ and $\sum_{i\,\text{labelled}}v_i^2=0$. By
Corollary~\ref{cor:nullspace}, $v^\top L v=0$ forces $v$ to be constant on each
connected component; and $\sum_{i\,\text{labelled}}v_i^2=0$ forces $v_i=0$ at
every labelled cell. Under the hypothesis each component contains a labelled
cell, so the constant value of $v$ on that component equals $0$; hence $v=0$ on
every component, i.e.\ $v=0$. Therefore $v^\top Gv>0$ for all $v\neq0$, i.e.\
$G\succ0$ and $G$ is invertible.

\emph{Uniqueness and optimality.} Because $J$ is convex with Hessian (in each
column) $2G\succ0$, it is strictly convex, so its unique global minimiser is the
unique solution of $\nabla_F J=0$, namely
$F^\star=\mu G^{-1}MY=\mu(L+\mu M)^{-1}MY$ from \eqref{eq:normal}. By
construction $F^\star$ minimises the Dirichlet energy
$\operatorname{Tr}(F^\top L F)$ among all $F$ subject to the soft fidelity
constraint weighted by $\mu$, which is the claimed minimum-energy extension.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Non-identifiability of a label-free component]
\label{cor:nonident}
If some connected component $C$ of the graph contains no labelled cell, then
$L+\mu M$ is singular and the minimiser of \eqref{eq:objective} is \emph{not}
unique: any minimiser $F^\star$ may be shifted by an arbitrary constant
(per-class) vector supported on $C$ without changing $J$. Equivalently, the
labels on a label-free component are not statistically identified by graph
smoothing alone.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Let $\mathbf{1}_C$ be the indicator of $C$ and $\mathbf{1}_C e_\ell^\top$ the
$n\times c$ matrix that places $\mathbf{1}_C$ in class column $\ell$. Since $C$ is
a union-free component, $\mathbf{1}_C\in\ker L$ by Corollary~\ref{cor:nullspace};
and since $C$ has no labelled cell, $M\mathbf{1}_C=0$. Hence
$(L+\mu M)\mathbf{1}_C=0$, so $L+\mu M$ is singular with $\mathbf{1}_C$ in its
kernel. If $F^\star$ solves \eqref{eq:normal}, then for any
$t\in\mathbb{R}$ the matrix $F^\star+t\,\mathbf{1}_C e_\ell^\top$ also solves it,
because $(L+\mu M)(t\mathbf{1}_C e_\ell^\top)=0$; and it attains the same
objective value, since both the Dirichlet term (the added field is component-wise
constant, zero energy) and the fidelity term (no labelled cell in $C$) are
unchanged. Thus the minimiser is non-unique on $C$.
\end{proof}

Corollary~\ref{cor:nonident} is the formal statement of the limitation the paper
foregrounds: smoothing cannot invent a label class that is unreachable from the
annotated set. It is exactly the failure mode that the active-sampling experiment
attacks by spending its budget in sparse, uncertain regions
(Fig.~\ref{fig:forecast}c), and it is the mechanism behind the null rare-state
recall on the time split, where the rare late-tip cells lie beyond the labelled
training distribution (Table~\ref{tab:main}).

\paragraph{The normalised iteration the code runs.}
The implementation does not solve \eqref{eq:normal} directly; it runs the
normalised diffusion of Zhou et al.~\cite{zhou2004local}. We prove that this
iteration is well posed, converges geometrically, and returns a closed-form fixed
point that is itself the unique minimiser of a normalised energy.

\begin{theorem}[Spectrum of the normalised adjacency]
\label{thm:spectrum}
Assume every cell has positive degree, $d_i>0$, and set
$S=D^{-1/2}AD^{-1/2}$. Then $S$ is symmetric and every eigenvalue $\lambda$ of $S$
satisfies $-1\le\lambda\le1$. Consequently, for any clamping parameter
$\alpha\in(0,1)$, the matrix $I-\alpha S$ is symmetric positive definite and hence
invertible, with eigenvalues in $[1-\alpha,1+\alpha]\subset(0,2)$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Symmetry of $S$ is immediate since $A=A^\top$ and $D^{-1/2}$ is diagonal. For the
spectral bound, note $S$ is similar to the random-walk matrix $P=D^{-1}A$ via
$S=D^{1/2}PD^{-1/2}$, so $S$ and $P$ share eigenvalues; we bound them on $S$
directly. The normalised Laplacian is
$\mathcal{L}=I-S=D^{-1/2}LD^{-1/2}$, which is congruent to $L\succeq0$
(Lemma~\ref{lem:dirichlet}) through the invertible $D^{-1/2}$, hence
$\mathcal{L}\succeq0$; this gives $S=I-\mathcal{L}\preceq I$, i.e.\ $\lambda\le1$
for every eigenvalue $\lambda$ of $S$. For the lower bound, consider
$I+S=D^{-1/2}(D+A)D^{-1/2}$, where $D+A$ is the \emph{signless} Laplacian; for any
$f$, $f^\top(D+A)f=\tfrac12\sum_{i,j}A_{ij}(f_i+f_j)^2\ge0$ by the same expansion
as in Lemma~\ref{lem:dirichlet} (replace the minus by a plus and use
$A_{ij}\ge0$). Hence $D+A\succeq0$, so $I+S\succeq0$ by congruence, giving
$\lambda\ge-1$. Therefore every eigenvalue of $S$ lies in $[-1,1]$. For
$\alpha\in(0,1)$, the eigenvalues of $I-\alpha S$ are $1-\alpha\lambda\in
[1-\alpha,\,1+\alpha]$, which are strictly positive; since $I-\alpha S$ is
symmetric with positive eigenvalues it is positive definite and invertible.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Geometric convergence and closed form of label propagation]
\label{thm:fixedpoint}
Under the hypotheses of Theorem~\ref{thm:spectrum}, fix $\alpha\in(0,1)$, let
$F_0$ be the one-hot seed (labels on annotated cells, zero elsewhere), and run the
iteration
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:iter}
F^{(t+1)}=\alpha\,S\,F^{(t)}+(1-\alpha)\,F_0,\qquad F^{(0)}=F_0.
\end{equation}
Then for every initialisation the iterates converge to the unique fixed point
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:closedform}
F^\star=(1-\alpha)\,(I-\alpha S)^{-1}F_0,
\end{equation}
with the geometric error bound
$\|F^{(t)}-F^\star\|_F\le(\alpha)^{t}\,\|F^{(0)}-F^\star\|_F$. Moreover $F^\star$
is the unique minimiser of the normalised graph-regularised energy
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:zhouenergy}
Q(F)=\tfrac12\,\operatorname{Tr}\!\big(F^\top(I-S)F\big)
+\tfrac{1-\alpha}{2\alpha}\,\|F-F_0\|_F^2 .
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
\emph{Fixed point.} A fixed point $F^\star$ of \eqref{eq:iter} satisfies
$F^\star=\alpha S F^\star+(1-\alpha)F_0$, i.e.\ $(I-\alpha S)F^\star=(1-\alpha)F_0$.
By Theorem~\ref{thm:spectrum} $I-\alpha S$ is invertible, so the unique fixed
point is $F^\star=(1-\alpha)(I-\alpha S)^{-1}F_0$, which is \eqref{eq:closedform}.

\emph{Geometric convergence.} Subtract the fixed-point relation from
\eqref{eq:iter}:
\[
F^{(t+1)}-F^\star=\alpha S\big(F^{(t)}-F^\star\big).
\]
Iterating, $F^{(t)}-F^\star=(\alpha S)^{t}\big(F^{(0)}-F^\star\big)$. Taking
Frobenius norms and using submultiplicativity with the spectral norm,
$\|F^{(t)}-F^\star\|_F\le\|(\alpha S)^t\|_2\,\|F^{(0)}-F^\star\|_F
=\alpha^{t}\,\|S\|_2^{t}\,\|F^{(0)}-F^\star\|_F$. Since $S$ is symmetric with
eigenvalues in $[-1,1]$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:spectrum}), $\|S\|_2=\max|\lambda|\le1$,
so $\|F^{(t)}-F^\star\|_F\le\alpha^{t}\|F^{(0)}-F^\star\|_F$. As $\alpha\in(0,1)$,
$\alpha^{t}\to0$, giving convergence from any initialisation at the geometric rate
$\alpha$; the finitely-many ($30$) iterations the code runs therefore approximate
$F^\star$ to error $\le\alpha^{30}$ of the initial gap.

\emph{Variational characterisation.} Expand $Q$ in \eqref{eq:zhouenergy} using
$I-S\succeq0$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:spectrum}); it is a sum of two convex quadratics,
strictly convex because the second term has Hessian
$\tfrac{1-\alpha}{\alpha}I\succ0$. Its gradient is
\[
\nabla_F Q=(I-S)F+\tfrac{1-\alpha}{\alpha}(F-F_0).
\]
Setting $\nabla_F Q=0$ and multiplying through by $\tfrac{\alpha}{1}$ gives
$\alpha(I-S)F+(1-\alpha)(F-F_0)=0$, i.e.\ $\big(\alpha I-\alpha S+(1-\alpha)I\big)F
=(1-\alpha)F_0$, which simplifies to $(I-\alpha S)F=(1-\alpha)F_0$. This is
exactly the fixed-point equation, so its unique solution $F^\star$ of
\eqref{eq:closedform} is the unique minimiser of $Q$. Hence the deterministic
power iteration the code runs converges to the unique minimiser of a normalised
Dirichlet-plus-fidelity energy, the normalised counterpart of
\eqref{eq:objective}.
\end{proof}

Theorem~\ref{thm:fixedpoint} certifies that the $30$-step, $\alpha=0.9$ diffusion
reported in Methods is a faithful, geometrically-convergent surrogate for the
closed-form estimator; it is the propagation step whose held-out accuracy and
rare-state recall appear in Table~\ref{tab:main} and Fig.~\ref{fig:forecast}a,b.
The active-sampling policy of Fig.~\ref{fig:forecast}c then uses the entropy of
this same forecast as its uncertainty signal, combined additively (after
$z$-scoring) with the inverse local density that flags the low-density pockets
where, by Corollary~\ref{cor:nonident}, smoothing alone is uninformative.

\section{Discussion}
Within a controlled synthetic benchmark, conditioning single-cell forecasts on the connectivity of
the cell $k$NN graph helps. Label propagation over the graph improves held-out accuracy consistently
across four leakage-checked transfer protocols, by roughly four to nine percentage points, and---more
usefully---it recovers a rare cell state that an otherwise-matched point classifier almost entirely
misses. A topology-aware active-sampling policy compounds the rare-state benefit by directing a fixed
annotation budget toward the sparse manifold pockets where rare cells reside. These results are
consistent with the long-standing intuition behind graph-based semi-supervised learning
\cite{zhou2004local,zhu2002labelprop} and with the central role of the $k$NN graph in single-cell
analysis \cite{wolf2018scanpy,haghverdi2016diffusion,saelens2019trajectory,luecken2019bestpractices}.

The gains are real but should not be oversold, and two boundaries deserve emphasis. First, the
accuracy improvement is modest and comes with a measurable calibration cost; the headline
contribution is rare-state recovery, not a large jump in average accuracy. Second, and more
fundamentally, the data are synthetic. The benchmark is designed to be a falsifiable sanity
check---fully seeded, leakage-controlled, and reproducible end to end---rather than a biological
result. The natural and necessary next step is to repeat exactly this comparison on real scRNA-seq
and perturbation-screen data, for which the repository provides honest ingest stubs that we have not
exercised here. The same protocol could then be strengthened with a learned message-passing
propagator \cite{kipf2017gcn} in place of fixed-point label propagation, and with calibration-aware
training to remove the ECE penalty.

\subsection{Limitations}
We list the limitations plainly because they bound every claim above.
\textbf{(1) Synthetic data.} All numbers come from a generated branching lineage, not from
biological measurements. We make no biological discovery claim, and the magnitudes reported here need
not transfer to real scRNA-seq, where noise, dropout and confounding are far more severe.
\textbf{(2) Modest accuracy gains, with a transductive confound.} The accuracy improvement over the
baseline is on the order of \numrange{0.04}{0.09} per split (mean \num{0.0595}), but this gap is
confounded: the propagation forecaster is transductive (graph built over all cells, held-out
connectivity seen at inference) while the $k$NN baseline is inductive (training cells only), so an
unquantified share of the gain reflects this information asymmetry rather than diffusion
(Sec.~\ref{sec:confound}). We treat \num{0.0595} as an upper bound and identify a matched inductive
graph control as the decisive missing experiment. The strongest effect is on rare-state recall, and
even that is null on the time split.
\textbf{(3) Calibration cost.} Graph smoothing worsens ECE on all four splits, so the predicted
probabilities are less trustworthy as confidences without recalibration.
\textbf{(4) ``Topology'' is shallow.} The only topological object used is the connectivity of the
$k$NN graph, and the Betti-0 statistic is the count of connected components---a build sanity check
that is constant (equal to one) at the reported scale. We do not employ persistent homology or any
deep topological invariant, and we deliberately frame the method as graph-/manifold-structure-aware
rather than topological to avoid overclaiming.
\textbf{(5) Wide intervals on rare metrics.} Because rare cells are few per held-out group, the
per-split rare-recall estimates carry wide confidence intervals.
\textbf{(6) Single benchmark family.} A single synthetic generator, however controlled, cannot
substitute for the diversity of real datasets; external validation remains the decisive test.

\section{Methods}

\paragraph{Synthetic lineage.}
Cells are generated in an $n_\text{features}$-dimensional feature space along a small tree of three
branches (a trunk progenitor and two fates that split at pseudotime $t_0=0.45$). Branch means drift
from fixed, well-separated anchors with progression in pseudotime; each cell is the branch mean plus
isotropic Gaussian noise of standard deviation \texttt{noise}. Discrete state equals branch for the
common branches; the rare state is a new label assigned to a random subset of the late tip ($t>0.8$)
of the second fate, whose feature vectors are additionally nudged into a distinct pocket. Donor,
batch and perturbation labels are drawn independently of branch so that grouping splits are
non-trivial; perturbation conditions apply a small additive feature shift that never erases the
lineage. The full-scale configuration is $n_\text{cells}=3000$, $n_\text{features}=16$,
$n_\text{branches}=3$, $n_\text{donors}=8$, $n_\text{batches}=4$, $n_\text{perturbations}=5$,
$\texttt{noise}=0.7$, $\texttt{rare\_fraction}=0.04$, $k=20$, $\texttt{label\_fraction}=0.03$,
$\texttt{active\_budget}=60$, run over $n_\text{seeds}=5$ seeds. Generation is fully seeded and
bit-for-bit reproducible.

\paragraph{Cell $k$NN graph and Betti-0.}
A symmetric $k$NN graph is built on cell features by connecting each cell to its $k$ nearest
neighbours (self excluded) and symmetrising the directed edges (either-direction convention), exactly
as standard single-cell pipelines do before clustering or pseudotime \cite{wolf2018scanpy}. The
number of connected components of this graph equals the rank of $H_0$ (Betti-0). Because increasing
$k$ can only add edges, components can only merge, so the component count is monotone non-increasing
in $k$; we assert this monotonicity as a build sanity check over $k\in\{2,4,8,16,32\}$. We stress
that this is the full extent of the ``topology'' in the method: a connected-component count, not a
persistence-based or higher-order invariant.

\paragraph{Forecasters.}
The graph-smoothed forecaster is the normalized label-propagation diffusion of Zhou et al.\
\cite{zhou2004local}. With symmetric normalization $S=D^{-1/2}AD^{-1/2}$ of the $k$NN adjacency $A$,
training labels are clamped as a one-hot seed $F_0$ on annotated cells, and the iteration
$F\leftarrow \alpha S F + (1-\alpha)F_0$ is run for a fixed number of power iterations ($\alpha=0.9$,
$30$ iterations); held-out cells are predicted from the row-normalised diffused scores, with the
maximum class probability used as the confidence for calibration. The baseline is a plain $k$NN
classifier in feature space fitted on the same annotated cells with the same $k$, i.e.\ identical
neighbourhood information \emph{without} diffusion. This control matches the neighbourhood radius but,
importantly, is \emph{inductive} (fit on training cells only) whereas the propagation forecaster is
\emph{transductive} (it builds the graph over all cells and so sees the held-out connectivity at
inference). The comparison therefore isolates diffusion only up to this transductive-versus-inductive
asymmetry, which we flag as a confound on the headline accuracy gap (Sec.~\ref{sec:confound}); a
matched inductive graph control is the needed next experiment.

\paragraph{Splits and annotation.}
Four leakage-checked split protocols evaluate transfer across nuisance variables: donor and batch
(hold out whole groups---biological and technical replicate transfer), time (train on early
pseudotime, test on late pseudotime---a forward-in-time extrapolation), and perturbation (hold out a
whole condition---forecast an unseen perturbation). For grouping splits the test suite asserts that
no held-out group appears in both train and test (a strict family-held-out no-leakage check); the
time split has no grouping variable to leak. Only a small fraction (\texttt{label\_fraction}) of the
train pool is annotated, with a light stratification that guarantees a few labels for each present
state including the rare one, mirroring how analysts annotate a handful of cells and propagate the
rest.

\paragraph{Active sampling.}
Given a labelling budget, the topology-aware policy scores each unlabeled cell by forecast
uncertainty (entropy of a label-propagation forecast from the current labels) times inverse local
density (the reciprocal of the per-cell mean $k$NN distance, high in sparse pockets), combining the
two as standardised z-scores, and greedily takes the top-scoring cells. The baseline is uniform
random querying. We measure rare-state recall on the still-unlabeled cells after the query and the
fraction of queried cells that were rare.

\paragraph{Metrics and aggregation.}
Accuracy is the fraction of held-out cells classified correctly. Expected calibration error is the
population-weighted gap between mean confidence and empirical accuracy over ten confidence bins. Rare-state
recall is the fraction of true rare cells recovered (returning $1$ by convention when a test set
contains no rare cells, so a split is never spuriously penalised). All metrics are averaged over $5$
seeds and reported with $95\%$ confidence intervals ($1.96\,\text{s.e.}$). All computation is CPU-only
(NumPy, SciPy, scikit-learn, NetworkX, Matplotlib); a GPU build is a drop-in but not required.

\paragraph{Experimental design and statistics.}
Each of the four split protocols is evaluated over the same $n_\text{seeds}=\num{5}$ master seeds
(seed $0$ controls lineage generation, annotation and the random query baseline), giving paired
graph-smoothed and $k$NN measurements per seed and split. Point estimates are seed means; dispersion
is the sample standard deviation $s$ with $\text{ddof}=1$, and the reported uncertainty is the
\SI{95}{\percent} normal-approximation confidence interval $1.96\,s/\sqrt{n}$ that supplies every
error bar in Figs.~\ref{fig:forecast} and the $\pm$ columns of Tables~\ref{tab:main}
and~\ref{tab:active}. We deliberately do not claim statistical significance on the per-split
rare-recall estimates: with $\sim$\SI{4}{\percent} rare cells the per-group rare counts are small,
the seed-level intervals are correspondingly wide (up to $\pm\num{0.3358}$ on the perturbation
split, Table~\ref{tab:main}), and the appropriate reading is the consistent direction of effect
rather than a thresholded $p$-value. Three integrity gates are asserted before any metric is

\noindent\textit{A from-scratch derivation of every concept the accompanying code implements.}
This appendix is written to be read top-to-bottom by someone who has seen linear
algebra and basic probability but \emph{not} single-cell biology, graph-based
semi-supervised learning, the graph Laplacian, label propagation, calibration or
active learning. Every symbol used in the reference implementation
(\texttt{topocell}) is defined, and each subsection names the source file it
maps to, so theory and implementation can be read side by side. The Methods
section above gives the condensed version; here we expand it pedagogically
without restating the experimental protocol.

\subsection{The problem in one paragraph}
A single-cell experiment measures thousands of individual cells, each described
by a vector of features (after dimensionality reduction, a low-dimensional
embedding of its gene-expression profile). Cells do not fill this space
uniformly: they lie near a low-dimensional curved surface (a ``manifold'') that
traces differentiation, activation or treatment response. The task we study is
\emph{forecasting}: predict the discrete state of a cell under a condition we did
not directly observe---a donor not yet profiled, a later technical batch, a later
point along the trajectory, or an untested perturbation. Two facts make this
hard. (i) Only a handful of cells are ever annotated by hand; the rest are
unlabelled. (ii) The biologically decisive states (transient progenitors,
treatment-resistant clones) are often \emph{rare}, and ordinary classifiers
trained to minimise average error ignore them. The scientific question of this
paper, kept deliberately narrow and falsifiable: does conditioning a forecaster
on the \emph{connectivity} of the cell $k$-nearest-neighbour ($k$NN) graph---rather
than on point features alone---improve (a) generalisation to held-out groups and
(b) recovery of a rare state? The answer is yes, but modestly on accuracy, more
clearly on rare-state recall, and at a small calibration cost; all data are
synthetic.

\subsection{The data: a seeded synthetic branching lineage}
\textit{Source: \texttt{src/topocell/synthetic.py}.}
We do not ship or fabricate real biological data (see \texttt{ingest.py} for
honest hooks into real AnnData). Instead we \emph{generate} a fully controlled
lineage with the same structure the method must handle, so every claim is
checkable bit-for-bit.

A ``lineage'' is a tree of branches over a continuous coordinate $t\in[0,1]$
called \emph{pseudotime} (how far a cell has progressed). The tree here has three
branches (\texttt{\_TREE} in \texttt{synthetic.py}): branch $0$ is the
trunk/common progenitor, living over $t\in[0,1]$; branch $1$ is the first fate,
which splits off the trunk at $t_0=0.45$; and branch $2$ is the second fate,
which also splits at $t_0=0.45$. Each branch has a fixed, well-separated
\emph{anchor} point in feature space. A cell on branch $b$ at pseudotime $t$ has
a mean feature vector $\mu(b,t)$ that drifts linearly from the split point toward
that branch's anchor (function \texttt{\_branch\_mean}). The cell itself is
\[
    x = \mu(b,t) + \varepsilon,\qquad \varepsilon\sim\mathcal{N}(0,\texttt{noise}^2 I).
\]
The key geometry: early cells (small $t$) of different fates share the same trunk
mean, so they \emph{overlap} and are hard to separate, whereas late cells are
well resolved. This is exactly the regime in which letting a label diffuse along
the manifold should beat a point classifier.

Each cell also carries the metadata a real forecaster would use: \texttt{state}
(the discrete label to predict, equal to the branch for the common branches);
\texttt{donor} (a biological-replicate id); \texttt{batch} (a technical-replicate
id); \texttt{perturbation} (a condition id, with $0$ the control, applying a small
additive feature shift that never erases the lineage); and \texttt{pseudotime}
(the continuous progression coordinate). Donor, batch and perturbation are drawn
\emph{independently} of the biological branch, so the held-out splits of
Section~\ref*{si:splits} are non-trivial (a donor spans multiple branches).

\paragraph{The rare state.} A brand-new state label (id $=n_\text{branches}$) is
carved out of the late tip ($t>0.8$) of the second fate: a random
\texttt{rare\_fraction} of cells there are relabelled and additionally
\emph{nudged} into their own pocket of feature space ($X[\text{chosen}]\mathrel{+}=
[1.5,1.0,0,\dots]$). With $\texttt{rare\_fraction}=0.04$ this is $\sim$\SI{4}{\percent}
of cells---the transient subpopulation an analyst most wants to find. Everything
is seeded with NumPy's \texttt{default\_rng(seed)}, so the lineage is bit-for-bit
reproducible across machines.

\subsection{The graph object: the \texorpdfstring{$k$}{k}NN cell graph}
\textit{Source: \texttt{src/topocell/graph.py}.}
The bridge from ``points in feature space'' to ``topology'' is the
$k$-nearest-neighbour graph, the same construction SCANPY uses before clustering
or pseudotime \cite{wolf2018scanpy}. To build it (\texttt{build\_knn\_graph}): for
each cell, find its $k$ nearest cells by Euclidean distance (self excluded),
giving a \emph{directed} $k$NN (an edge $i\to j$ if $j$ is among $i$'s $k$
neighbours); then \emph{symmetrise} with the either-direction convention
$A\leftarrow\max(A,A^\top)$, so an undirected edge $\{i,j\}$ exists if $i$ picked
$j$ \emph{or} $j$ picked $i$. Symmetrising is the standard single-cell convention
and makes connected components well defined. The result is a symmetric adjacency
matrix $A$ ($A=A^\top$, $A_{ii}=0$). $A$ is the graph, and it is the
\emph{substrate} for everything that follows: labels diffuse over its edges, and
rare cells are hunted in its sparse regions. The \emph{degree} of cell $i$ is
$d_i=\sum_j A_{ij}$ (how many neighbours it has); collect the degrees on the
diagonal of $D=\operatorname{diag}(d_1,\dots,d_n)$.

\subsection{Topology: Betti-0 as a connectivity diagnostic}
\textit{Source: \texttt{graph.py}.}
The only topological invariant the method uses is Betti-0, the number of connected
components of the graph, equal to the rank of the $0$-th homology group $H_0$
(\texttt{n\_connected\_components} computes it with SciPy's
\texttt{connected\_components}). Intuitively, a connected component is a maximal
set of cells reachable from one another by walking along edges; the number of them
counts how many disjoint pieces the data splits into. A real, testable property:
as $k$ grows, edges are only \emph{added}, so components can only \emph{merge}---the
component count is monotone non-increasing in $k$. \texttt{betti0\_curve} returns
this count for $k\in\{2,4,8,16,32\}$, and \texttt{tests/test\_betti\_monotonic.py}
asserts the monotonicity as a build sanity check. Honesty note: at the reported
scale the graph is a single connected component for every $k$ in that set (the
Betti-0 curve is constant at $1$), so Betti-0 here is a \emph{passive} sanity
check, not an informative descriptor---which is precisely why the paper frames the
method as graph-/manifold-structure-aware, not as deep topology (no persistent
homology, no higher-order invariants). \texttt{topology\_features} and
\texttt{local\_density} add cheap graph-level descriptors (mean degree, clustering,
and a per-cell inverse-density proxy) used only by the active-sampling policy of
Section~\ref*{si:active}.

\subsection{The graph Laplacian and Dirichlet energy}
\label{si:laplacian}
This is the heart of the mathematics. Define the (combinatorial) graph Laplacian
$L=D-A$. Its single most important property is that, for any vector $f$ on the
cells,
\[
    f^\top L f = \tfrac12\sum_{i,j} A_{ij}\,(f_i-f_j)^2 \;\ge\; 0.
\]
This is the \emph{Dirichlet energy}: it is small exactly when $f$ varies smoothly
across edges (neighbouring cells get similar values) and large when $f$ jumps
across edges. $L$ is symmetric positive semidefinite; the all-ones vector lies in
its null space (constant labels cost zero energy), and the multiplicity of the
eigenvalue $0$ equals the number of connected components (tying back to Betti-0).
``Smoothing labels over the graph'' will mean choosing $f$ that is smooth in this
Dirichlet sense while still matching the few labels we have, made precise next.

\subsection{Label propagation as a minimum-Dirichlet-energy label extension}
\label{si:labelprop}
\textit{Source: \texttt{src/topocell/policy.py}.}
We observe one-hot labels $Y$ on a small labelled set (mask $M$ with $M_{ii}=1$ if
cell $i$ is labelled, else $0$) and want a soft-label matrix $F$ (rows are cells,
columns are classes). The variational objective---stated as
Theorem~\ref{thm:label-prop} above---is
\[
    J(F)=\operatorname{Tr}(F^\top L F)+\mu\,\|M(F-Y)\|_F^2 .
\]
The first term is the Dirichlet energy of Section~\ref{si:laplacian} (smoothness
on the graph); the second is a \emph{fidelity} penalty (stay near the observed
labels on labelled cells), with $\mu>0$ the trade-off. $J$ is convex; setting the
gradient to zero gives
\[
    2LF+2\mu M(F-Y)=0 \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; (L+\mu M)F=\mu M Y .
\]
On any connected component containing at least one labelled cell, $L+\mu M$ is
positive definite, so the minimiser is \emph{unique}: label propagation is the
unique minimum-Dirichlet-energy extension of the observed labels. The same theorem
states the limitation---a component with no labels is not identified by smoothing
alone---which is exactly the failure mode that active sampling
(Section~\ref{si:active}) attacks.

\paragraph{The actual iteration.} The implementation runs the normalised form of
Zhou et al.\ \cite{zhou2004local}. Symmetrically normalise the adjacency,
$S=D^{-1/2}AD^{-1/2}$ (eigenvalues in $[-1,1]$); seed $F_0$ as one-hot labels on
labelled cells and zero elsewhere; and iterate
\[
    F \;\leftarrow\; \alpha\,(S F) + (1-\alpha)\,F_0 .
\]
This is a damped power iteration whose fixed point
$F^\star=(1-\alpha)(I-\alpha S)^{-1}F_0$ is the closed-form solution of a
normalised version of $J(F)$, with $\alpha$ the diffusion/clamping trade-off. The
code uses $\alpha=0.9$ and $30$ power iterations (deterministic, CPU, no learning).
Held-out cells are predicted by the argmax of their row-normalised diffused
scores; the maximum class probability is the \emph{confidence} used for
calibration (Section~\ref{si:metrics}). This fixed-point solver is a faithful
surrogate for a message-passing GNN: dropping in a learned propagator
\cite{kipf2017gcn} leaves the rest of the pipeline unchanged.

\paragraph{The honest baseline.} The \texttt{KNNForecaster} is a plain
$k$-nearest-neighbour classifier in feature space, fitted on the \emph{same}
labelled cells with the \emph{same} $k$, but with \emph{no} diffusion. It sees
identical neighbourhood information; the difference is that it does not let
labels spread along the graph \emph{and} that it is inductive (fit on training
cells only), whereas \texttt{LabelPropagation} is transductive (it builds the
graph over all cells and so observes the held-out cells' connectivity at
inference). Comparing the two therefore measures diffusion only up to this
transductive-versus-inductive asymmetry, which inflates the headline accuracy gap;
a matched inductive graph control (e.g.\ label spreading on the training graph
with out-of-sample extension) is required to attribute the gain to diffusion alone
(main text, Sec.~\ref{sec:confound}).

\subsection{Splits, leakage and the few-label regime}
\label{si:splits}
\textit{Source: \texttt{src/topocell/splits.py}.}
A forecaster that only works when train and test share donors, batches, conditions
or timepoints is a \emph{memoriser}, not a forecaster. So we evaluate transfer
across nuisance variables under four protocols (\texttt{SPLIT\_TYPES}):
\texttt{donor} (hold out whole donors---biological-replicate transfer);
\texttt{batch} (hold out whole batches---technical-replicate transfer);
\texttt{time} (train on early pseudotime $t<\text{cutoff}$, test on late---a genuine
forward-in-time extrapolation); and \texttt{perturbation} (hold out a whole
condition---forecast an unseen perturbation). For grouping splits,
\texttt{has\_leakage()} checks that the held-out group's id appears in test but not
in train (disjoint group sets), and the test suite asserts this strict
family-held-out no-leakage property. The time split cuts on a continuous
coordinate, so there is no grouping variable to leak (clean by construction).

\paragraph{Few-label annotation.} Only a fraction \texttt{label\_fraction} of the
train pool is actually annotated (\texttt{\_annotate}), mirroring how analysts
label a handful of cells and propagate the rest. The selection is lightly
\emph{stratified}: a couple of labels are guaranteed for each present state,
including the rare one---with zero rare labels no method could recall the rare
state and the comparison would be uninformative rather than honest. The held-out
test cells are never annotated.

\subsection{Active sampling: hunting the rare state}
\label{si:active}
\textit{Sources: \texttt{policy.py}, \texttt{graph.py}.}
Active learning chooses \emph{which} cells to annotate next, given a fixed
labelling budget. The topology-aware policy (\texttt{active\_sample},
\texttt{strategy='topology'}) scores each unlabelled cell by
\[
    \text{score} = \operatorname{zscore}(\text{uncertainty}) + \operatorname{zscore}(\text{inverse local density}),
\]
and greedily takes the top \texttt{budget} cells. The two ingredients are: (i)
\emph{uncertainty}, the Shannon entropy $H(p)=-\sum_c p_c\log p_c$ of a
label-propagation forecast from the \emph{current} labels (\texttt{\_entropy}),
where high entropy means the model is unsure---near a decision boundary, the
classic active-learning signal; and (ii) \emph{inverse local density}, the
reciprocal of a cell's mean $k$NN distance (\texttt{graph.local\_density}), since
rare states sit in low-density pockets, making $1/\text{density}$ a cheap,
topology-aware ``where are the rare cells'' signal. Standardising each to a
z-score (subtract mean, divide by standard deviation) puts the two heterogeneous
signals on a common scale before adding them. The baseline is uniform
\emph{random} querying (\texttt{strategy='random'}). \texttt{recall\_after\_query}
measures rare-state recall on the still-unlabelled cells before versus after adding
the queried set; the gap is the active-sampling \emph{lift}. We also report the
fraction of queried cells that were genuinely rare (\texttt{rare\_query\_rate}).

\subsection{Metrics and aggregation}
\label{si:metrics}
\textit{Source: \texttt{src/topocell/metrics.py}.}
\emph{Accuracy} is the fraction of held-out cells classified correctly.
\emph{Expected calibration error} (ECE) asks whether the confidences are
trustworthy: bin the test cells by predicted confidence into ten bins, in each bin
compare mean confidence to empirical accuracy, and take the population-weighted
average gap; a perfectly calibrated forecaster has $\text{ECE}\to0$. We report ECE
precisely because graph smoothing sharpens probabilities and, in this setting,
makes them mildly overconfident---a small, honestly disclosed cost that
recalibration in the manner of Guo et al.\ \cite{guo2017calibration} would remove.
\emph{Rare-state recall} is the fraction of true rare cells the model labels rare;
by convention it returns $1$ when a test set has no rare cells (nothing to miss),
so a split is never spuriously penalised. \emph{Aggregation} (\texttt{summarize}):
every metric is computed per seed and reported as mean, sample standard deviation
$s$ (with $\text{ddof}=1$) and a $95\%$ confidence interval $1.96\,s/\sqrt{n}$ over
the $n=5$ seeds. These \texttt{ci95} values are exactly the error bars in the
figures and the $\pm$ in the text.

\subsection{What the result means (and what it does not)}
Within this controlled synthetic benchmark, conditioning forecasts on $k$NN-graph
connectivity helps: label propagation beats the matched point classifier on
held-out accuracy on all four splits (mean gain $\approx\num{0.0595}$, roughly four
to nine percentage points)---though this gap is confounded by the transductive
(propagation) versus inductive ($k$NN) asymmetry and so is an upper bound on the
diffusion-attributable gain (Sec.~\ref{sec:confound})---and---the clearer
effect---recovers a rare state the baseline almost never finds. The topology-aware active-sampling policy raises
rare-state recall from \num{0.0247} (random) to \num{0.3556}. These gains carry a
small, consistent calibration penalty (ECE is slightly worse on every split). The
accuracy gain is real but modest; the headline contribution is rare-state recovery,
and even that is null on the time split (forward-in-time extrapolation puts the
rare late-tip cells entirely outside the training distribution---reported as-is).
The critical caveats, expanded in the Limitations above, are that all data are
synthetic (no biological discovery claim); that the ``topology'' is shallow
($k$NN-graph connectivity and a Betti-0 component count that is constant at this
