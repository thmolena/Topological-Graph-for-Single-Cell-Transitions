\title{Directed spectral-truncated operators for inductive classification of quantum phases across a control-parameter sweep}

\author{Molena Huynh}
\email{molena.huynh@jmp.com}
\affiliation{North Carolina State University, Raleigh, North Carolina 27695, USA}
\date{\today}

\begin{abstract}
Read from the keyboard, a quantum simulator is a stochastic device: every preparation returns a single
measurement record, so identifying the phase it occupies is statistical inference from finite, noisy
samples, and doing it credibly demands calibrated uncertainty rather than a bare point label. Taking that
view literally, we introduce a spectral-truncated, drive-directed graph operator that inductively classifies
the phase of a parameterized quantum many-body system as a control parameter is swept across a transition,
confounded by device variation, shot noise, and the rarity of critical regimes. The symmetric normalized adjacency
$S=D^{-1/2}AD^{-1/2}$ used to propagate labels is reversible, discarding the directed arrow of the drive,
and, built over all states, consults held-out connectivity at inference. Our operator fixes both with three components: a non-self-adjoint forward propagator
transporting labels along the sweep, a band-limiting spectral truncation that denoises it into a closed-form
solve, and an out-of-sample Nystr\"om extension making the classifier inductive and transferable. The truncation transplants onto the state graph the
machinery of $C^*$-algebraic kernel machines, yielding a noncommutative operator $*$-algebra with a
positive-definite kernel. On a seeded synthetic benchmark ($n=\num{3000}$ states), it beats a matched inductive
$k$NN classifier across all four leakage-checked transfer protocols ($\num{0.6856}$ versus $\num{0.5992}$)
and recovers a rare critical regime the baseline almost never finds (recall $\num{0.3322}$ versus
$\num{0.0151}$), with its largest margin on forward extrapolation. A split-conformal layer adds distribution-free coverage against a $\num{0.90}$ target, and
temperature scaling cuts calibration error by \SI{73}{\percent}. All data are synthetic and the
pipeline is fully reproducible; validation on classical shadows from a programmable simulator is the next step.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
A quantum simulator is, from the keyboard, a stochastic device interrogated only by sampling: the Born rule
means each run returns a single bitstring drawn from a distribution, so everything one learns about the
prepared state is statistical. Recognizing the \emph{phase} a state occupies is therefore not a
deterministic readout but an inference problem---estimating a discrete label from a finite, noisy sample and
attaching honest uncertainty to it---and this statistical character, so often left implicit, is exactly what
determines whether a phase call can be trusted. Programmable quantum simulators now prepare and measure
many-body states across entire phase diagrams, and a central task in machine learning for quantum physics is
to identify the phase a state occupies from its measurement record---typically the randomized-measurement
data of classical shadows \cite{huang2020shadows,elben2023randomized}, themselves a randomized-measurement
statistic that compresses an exponentially large state into a tractable feature vector. Recognizing phases and locating transitions from such data is a workhorse of the
field \cite{carrasquilla2017ml,vannieuwenburg2017confusion,carleo2019rmp,biamonte2017qml,carrasquilla2020review},
spanning supervised classifiers, unsupervised discovery of order parameters by
clustering, principal-component analysis, and variational autoencoders
\cite{wang2016discovering,wetzel2017unsupervised}, diffusion-map detection of
topological order \cite{rodriguez2019topological}, and provably sample-efficient
phase classification from local measurements \cite{huang2022provable}, yet two
structural features of the data defeat the standard classifier. First, the shadow features of a
parameterized system concentrate near a low-dimensional manifold---the phase diagram---routinely
approximated by a $k$-nearest-neighbor ($k$NN) similarity graph before clustering, embedding, or kernel
learning
\cite{coifman2006diffusion,belkin2003laplacian,luxburg2007spectral,vandermaaten2008tsne,havlicek2019,schuld2019}---%
the non-Euclidean, graph-and-manifold geometry that geometric deep learning takes as its
domain \cite{bronstein2017geometric}.
Second, the physically decisive regimes---quantum critical points, narrow topological windows---are rare,
and classifiers trained to minimize average error miss them.

The canonical response is graph-based semi-supervised learning
\cite{zhu2002labelprop,zhou2004local,kipf2017gcn,scarselli2009gnn,belkin2006manifold}: phase labels diffuse over the state graph so that each state borrows
statistical strength from its neighborhood. The diffusion operator used almost universally is the symmetric
normalized adjacency $S=D^{-1/2}AD^{-1/2}$ and its Laplacian $L=D-A$ \cite{coifman2006diffusion}, and it
carries two defects that bear directly on classification across a sweep. (i) It is self-adjoint, hence
reversible: it cannot encode the directed arrow of the drive, even though the control-parameter sweep
supplies exactly that arrow and directed, non-Hermitian transition operators are the native language of
driven and open quantum dynamics \cite{chung2005directed,ashida2020nonhermitian,sachdev2011qpt,bergholtz2021exceptional}---
just as spectral learning on directed graphs has turned to complex Hermitian (magnetic-Laplacian) operators
that encode edge orientation in their phase \cite{zhang2021magnet}. (ii)
Built over all states, it is transductive: it consults the held-out states' connectivity at inference, so a
measured accuracy gain over an inductive point classifier conflates the value of diffusion with a
transductive information advantage---the confound that comparable studies flag as their decisive missing
control.

We turn both defects into design targets and introduce a spectral-truncated, drive-directed graph
operator that we develop into a falsifiable quantum-phase classifier. Rather than re-applying label
propagation, we contribute four innovations. (1) We construct a \emph{directed} propagator $P$ by
reweighting each edge $i\!\to\!j$ with a bounded forward kernel $\kappa(s_j-s_i)$ of the control-parameter
increment and row-normalizing; $P$ is non-self-adjoint (relative non-normality $\nu(P)=\num{0.0370}$)---the single state-graph instance of the operator
noncommutativity that recent $C^*$-algebraic kernel machines exploit for expressiveness
\cite{hashimoto2026spectral,vansuijlekom2021spectral,connes2021truncations}---and it transports labels
along the sweep rather than merely smoothing them across it. (2) We \emph{band-limit} the diffusion by
spectral truncation to the leading $r$ graph-Fourier modes, denoising a lazy directed walk into a
closed-form solve; this is precisely the spectral truncation of multiplication operators of noncommutative
geometry, and because the truncated product is \emph{not} the product of the truncations, it generates a
second, genuinely noncommutative structure with a positive-definite Gram kernel ($\lambda_{\min}=\num{1.62}>0$)---the state-graph instance
of a $C^*$-algebraic kernel. (3) We render the classifier \emph{inductive} through an out-of-sample
Nystr\"om extension \cite{williams2001nystrom,bengio2004oos}, so the matched inductive control is the
method itself rather than a missing experiment, and the trained operator transfers to states from a device
it never saw. (4) We calibrate the rare-regime prediction with a distribution-free \emph{conformal} layer
\cite{vovk2005algorithmic,lei2018distribution,guo2017calibration,angelopoulos2021gentle,romano2020aps,huang2023cfgnn}
and replace the passive
connected-component count of prior work with a genuine multiscale \emph{persistent-homology} descriptor
\cite{edelsbrunner2010computational,carlsson2009topology}, of the kind that already yields quantitative,
interpretable order parameters for phase transitions \cite{cole2021persistent}.

We evaluate this operator against the symmetric smoother and a matched point classifier under identical,
leakage-checked conditions. The directed, truncated, inductive operator improves held-out accuracy on every
transfer protocol (mean \num{0.6856} versus \num{0.5992}), recovers a rare critical regime the baseline
misses (rare-state recall \num{0.3322} versus \num{0.0151}), and---uniquely---rescues forward
extrapolation across the sweep ($+\num{0.1749}$ accuracy on the schedule split). All experiments run on a seeded synthetic benchmark that emulates the
shadow-feature geometry of a parameterized quantum system, a controlled stand-in rather than
quantum-device data; the result is falsifiable and fully reproducible, isolating exactly what the directed,
truncated, inductive operator buys and what it does and does not support. The analytical guarantees stated
below are proved in full in
Appendix~\ref{sec:theory}, and the statistical theory of the inductive classifier---generalization under the
covariate shift the sweep induces, consistency of the estimated operator, the truncation--estimation
trade-off, and the active design of the sweep---is developed and proved in
Appendix~\ref{sec:inductive-theory}; numerical and reproducibility details are collected in
Appendix~\ref{sec:methods}; and a self-contained, from-scratch derivation of every implemented component is
given in Appendix~\ref{sec:scratch}.

This work is the latest entry in a program that treats the spectral truncation of operators as a common
design primitive across quantum optimization, simulation, and error correction. The same primitive recurs
under graph-conditioned trust regions and uncertainty calibration for query-efficient quantum approximate
optimization \cite{huynh2026gctr,huynh2026gctrcalpro}, the measurement and query-budget
accounting of warm-started low-depth circuits \cite{huynh2026meascost,huynh2026certqb}, topology-conditioned
parameter transfer and operator-spectral priors for schedule reuse
\cite{huynh2026paramxferB,huynh2026ostqaoa,huynh2026topoqaoa}, commutator-graph and
Lie-algebraic truncation of product-formula error \cite{huynh2026topoham,huynh2026lie}, and the
transfer-operator, covariance-aware, and gauge-equivariant decoding of structured noise in quantum error
correction \cite{huynh2026gnndec,huynh2026topoqec,huynh2026covdec,huynh2026fiber}, and
structure-preserving learning of open-system dynamics \cite{huynh2026cptppinn}. The present manuscript
carries that truncation onto the state graph of a parameterized quantum system, where the band-limiting
projector $\Pi_r$ plays the role the residual or schedule truncation plays elsewhere in the series, and
realizes on it the spectral truncation of multiplication operators that the
operator-spectral prior of Ref.~\cite{huynh2026ostqaoa} uses for QAOA schedule transfer.

\section{The drive-directed, spectrally truncated operator}
\label{sec:method}
The classifier is a single non-self-adjoint operator (Fig.~\ref{fig:schematic}), and we develop it in the
order one would derive it: from states to a directed propagator, to its band-limited spectral truncation,
to the two distinct noncommutativities the construction creates, and finally to the inductive extension and
conformal layer that turn it into a usable, falsifiable classifier. Every analytical claim made along the
way is stated as a theorem and proved in full in Appendix~\ref{sec:theory}.

\subsection{From states to a directed propagator}
A symmetric $k$NN graph on shadow features gives the adjacency $A=A^\top$ that quantum-data pipelines
already build to approximate the state manifold \cite{coifman2006diffusion}; its degree is
$d_i=\sum_j A_{ij}$, $D=\operatorname{diag}(d_i)$, and the standard smoother is the self-adjoint
$S=D^{-1/2}AD^{-1/2}$. The arrow that $S$ discards is supplied by the control parameter. We therefore
reweight each directed edge $i\!\to\!j$ by a bounded forward kernel of the control-parameter increment,
\begin{equation}
W_{ij}=A_{ij}\,\kappa(s_j-s_i),\qquad \kappa(\delta)=\exp\!\big(\beta\tanh(\delta/\tau)\big),
\label{eq:kernel}
\end{equation}
and row-normalize to a transition matrix $P=D_W^{-1}W$ with $(D_W)_{ii}=\sum_j W_{ij}$. The kernel
up-weights forward edges ($s_j>s_i$) and down-weights backward ones, with $\beta\ge0$ setting the
directional strength and $\tau>0$ the control-parameter scale; the $\tanh$ keeps the reweighting bounded so
that no single edge dominates. Because forward and backward edges receive different weights, $P$ is
non-self-adjoint, $PP^\top\neq P^\top P$, and the scalar $\nu(P)=\|PP^\top-P^\top P\|_F/\|P\|_F^2$ witnesses
this non-normality [Theorem~\ref{thm:noncomm}(i)]. This is the property that lets $P$ transport labels along
the sweep rather than merely smooth them across it, and it is shared by the directed, non-Hermitian
transition operators that describe driven and open quantum dynamics
\cite{chung2005directed,ashida2020nonhermitian}.

\subsection{Spectral truncation: band-limiting the operator}
A directed walk on a noisy state graph still mixes high-frequency shot noise into the prediction. We
suppress it by spectral truncation. The graph-Fourier modes are the eigenvectors $u_1,\dots,u_n$ of $S$;
the $r$ with the largest eigenvalues are the smoothest, lowest-frequency functions on the graph
(equivalently the lowest modes of the normalized Laplacian $\mathcal L=I-S$). Collecting them in
$U_r=[u_1\cdots u_r]$ and writing $\Pi_r=U_rU_r^\top$ for the band-limiting projector, we compress a lazy
directed walk $\widetilde P=(1-\varepsilon)I+\varepsilon P$ to its action on the band-limited subspace,
\begin{equation}
B=U_r^\top\widetilde P\,U_r\in\mathbb{R}^{r\times r},
\label{eq:trunc}
\end{equation}
and solve the band-limited diffusion in closed form: seeding one-hot phase labels $F_0$ on the annotated
states, the field is $F^\star=U_r(I-\alpha B)^{-1}(1-\alpha)U_r^\top F_0$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:fixedpoint}).
Truncation does double duty. It denoises---restricting to the $r$ smoothest modes is, for the
positive-semidefinite diffusion filter the method applies, the Eckart--Young--Mirsky-optimal rank-$r$
approximation, and it maximizes the retained smoothing energy $\operatorname{Tr}(\Pi_r S)$
(Theorem~\ref{thm:trunc}). And it compresses---the solve runs in $r$ coordinates rather than $n$,
$r\ll n$.

\subsection{Two distinct noncommutativities}
Two structural facts about this operator are easily conflated, and only one of them is the
spectral-truncation noncommutativity we claim; we therefore separate them deliberately. The first is
directedness: $P$ is non-self-adjoint because the forward kernel makes incoming and outgoing transition
mass asymmetric at any state with a net control-parameter gradient [Theorem~\ref{thm:noncomm}(i)]. This is
the source of forward transport, but it is a property of any directed operator and is \emph{not} special to
truncation. The second is genuinely noncommutative and is created by truncation itself: for state-wise
functions $\phi,\psi$ the truncated multiplication operators
$M_\phi^{(r)}=U_r^\top\operatorname{diag}(\phi)U_r$ do not commute, $[M_\phi^{(r)},M_\psi^{(r)}]\neq0$,
vanishing only in the full-rank limit $\Pi_r=I$ [Theorem~\ref{thm:noncomm}(ii)]. This is precisely the
spectral truncation of multiplication operators in noncommutative geometry
\cite{vansuijlekom2021spectral,connes2021truncations}: the truncated multiplications generate a
noncommutative operator $*$-algebra whose symmetric Gram object $\mathcal K=\sum_c M_cM_c^\top$ is a
positive-definite kernel on the band-limited subspace (Theorem~\ref{thm:kernel})---the state-graph instance
of a $C^*$-algebraic kernel machine \cite{hashimoto2026spectral}. We emphasize that we instantiate this
structure on the state graph and supply the Gram kernel; we do not develop a new vector-valued
reproducing-kernel framework, and the truncation noncommutativity is a finite-rank object, not a full
$C^*$-algebraic construction.

\subsection{Making the classifier inductive and device-transferable}
A diffusion built over all states is transductive: its accuracy gain cannot be disentangled from the
held-out connectivity it consumes. We therefore remove the confound by construction rather than by a
separate control experiment. The inductive classifier builds $A$, $U_r$, and $P$ on the training states
only and labels a held-out state $x_\ast$ by a Gaussian-weighted average of the training field over its
nearest \emph{training} states, $\widehat F(x_\ast)=\sum_{i\in\mathcal N(x_\ast)}w_iF^\star_{\mathrm{tr}}(x_i)$%
---an out-of-sample Nystr\"om/Nadaraya--Watson extension \cite{williams2001nystrom,bengio2004oos} that never
forms a test--test edge and never reads held-out adjacency (Theorem~\ref{thm:inductive}). This is the same
transductive-to-inductive shift that node-embedding methods make when they learn to aggregate a node's
neighborhood rather than memorize per-node embeddings \cite{hamilton2017graphsage,velickovic2018gat}. The matched
inductive control is therefore the method itself, not an experiment we leave undone, and because inference
on a new state consults only the trained field, the operator transfers to shadow data from a device or
calibration it never saw. It is this inductive variant that we put forward throughout.

\subsection{Conformal calibration and a multiscale topology signal}
Two layers complete the classifier. A split-conformal layer holds out a calibration slice of the annotated
states, computes nonconformity scores $1-p_y$, and thresholds at the conformal quantile to form prediction
sets with finite-sample coverage $\ge1-\delta$ under exchangeability (Theorem~\ref{thm:conformal});
temperature scaling additionally recalibrates the scalar confidence \cite{guo2017calibration}. Separately,
we replace the constant connected-component count of prior work with the degree-$0$ persistence barcode of
the state cloud, computed exactly by union--find on the $k$NN distance graph
\cite{edelsbrunner2010computational,carlsson2009topology}; the resulting $\beta_0(\epsilon)$ curve is a
genuine multiscale descriptor of the phase diagram, and its per-state witness drives an active sampler that
targets the rare critical regime.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}
We evaluate the operator on a fully seeded synthetic benchmark that emulates the shadow-feature geometry of
a parameterized quantum system, comparing the inductive spectral-truncated directed-diffusion (STDD)
classifier---the method---against its transductive variant, symmetric label propagation, and a matched
inductive $k$NN baseline under four leakage-checked transfer protocols. Every reported number is generated
verbatim from the run artifact \texttt{results/summary.json} (Appendix~\ref{sec:methods}); none is typed by
hand.

\subsection{A controlled phase sweep with device, noise, and control-parameter structure}
Our benchmark reproduces the device, calibration, and control-parameter structure a real phase classifier
must handle while remaining bit-for-bit reproducible (Figs.~\ref{fig:schematic} and \ref{fig:sweep}).
States live in a low-dimensional shadow-feature space---a stand-in for the classical-shadow or
randomized-measurement features of a parameterized many-body system---and trace a phase diagram of three
phases (a disordered parent phase and two ordered phases that bifurcate at the critical control parameter
$s_0=0.45$) as Gaussian clusters whose centroid drifts with $s$; states near the transition overlap and are
hard to separate, while deep-phase states are well resolved. Each state carries the metadata a classifier
would use---phase, control parameter, device, shot-noise batch, and Hamiltonian-perturbation condition---%
assigned independently of the underlying phase so that the held-out splits are nontrivial. One regime is
carved from the late tip of the second ordered phase to be a deliberately rare critical window, comprising
\SI{4}{\percent} of states. The full-scale configuration generates \num{3000} states with \num{16} shadow
features, \num{8} devices, \num{4} shot-noise batches, and \num{5} Hamiltonian-perturbation conditions over
\num{5} random seeds; the run completed in \SI{32.0}{\second} with a peak memory of \SI{621.2}{\mega\byte}.
Across seeds the integrity gates passed: the grouping splits were leakage-free, the rare regime was
present, and the directed propagator was non-normal.

\subsection{The operator is genuinely noncommutative, and truncation is well posed}
We begin by verifying the two structural properties our analysis requires of the operator---genuine
non-normality and a well-posed truncation (Fig.~\ref{fig:operator}). The directed propagator is non-normal:
its relative non-normality is $\nu(P)=\num{0.0370}$, and directional reweighting strictly increases it over
the undirected random walk [$\nu(P_0)=\num{0.0272}$, itself nonzero because degree-asymmetric row
normalization already breaks normality], so the arrow of the drive contributes a measurable
\SI{36}{\percent} of additional non-normality [Fig.~\ref{fig:operator}(a)]. This directedness is distinct
from the truncation noncommutativity, which is a separate and genuinely noncommutative object: the
truncated multiplication operators satisfy $\|[M_\phi^{(r)},M_\psi^{(r)}]\|_F=\num{5.93}$
[Theorem~\ref{thm:noncomm}(ii)], and the Gram kernel $\mathcal K=\sum_c M_cM_c^\top$ they generate has
smallest eigenvalue $\lambda_{\min}=\num{1.62}>0$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:kernel}), so it is strictly positive
definite. The graph-Fourier spectrum of $S$ decays smoothly from $1$ to $\approx\num{0.41}$ over the first
\num{80} modes [Fig.~\ref{fig:operator}(b)], so a rank-$r$ truncation retains the low-frequency,
phase-relevant structure while discarding high-frequency shot noise; sweeping the rank reveals an interior
operating point---accuracy is flat near $\approx\num{0.71}$ while rare-state recall peaks around
$r\in[64,80]$ [Fig.~\ref{fig:operator}(c)], the bias--variance trade-off that Theorem~\ref{thm:trunc}
formalizes. We fix $r=\num{80}$ throughout, at which the diffusion is well posed (spectral radius
$\rho(\alpha B)=\num{0.9209}<1$, Theorem~\ref{thm:fixedpoint}).

\subsection{The inductive operator beats a matched point classifier on every protocol}
Under all four leakage-checked protocols, the inductive STDD classifier predicts held-out phases more
accurately than the inductive $k$NN baseline that receives the same annotated states and the same
neighborhood radius [Table~\ref{tab:main}, Fig.~\ref{fig:forecast}(a)]. Accuracy rises on the device split
from \num{0.6550} to \num{0.7227} ($+\num{0.0677}$), on the shot-noise split from \num{0.6355} to
\num{0.7134} ($+\num{0.0779}$), on the Hamiltonian split from \num{0.6490} to \num{0.6741}
($+\num{0.0251}$), and on the schedule split---forward extrapolation across the sweep, the hardest
protocol---from \num{0.4574} to \num{0.6323} ($+\num{0.1749}$). Averaged over the four splits the inductive
operator reaches \num{0.6856} against \num{0.5992} for the baseline, a mean gain of \num{0.0864}, and it
improves on \emph{every} split rather than on average only. The comparison is inductive on both sides: the
STDD field is built on the training states and extended to held-out states by the Nystr\"om step
(Theorem~\ref{thm:inductive}), so the gain is not the transductive-versus-inductive asymmetry that inflates
symmetric-smoothing studies. Strikingly, the same inductive operator even exceeds the transductive
symmetric label-propagation baseline on three of four splits and in the mean (\num{0.6856} versus
\num{0.6587}), despite operating in the strictly harder inductive regime---and despite that baseline seeing
the held-out connectivity it does not.

\subsection{Directional transport recovers the critical regime and rescues forward extrapolation}
The operator's sharpest effect is on the rare critical regime and on the forward direction of the sweep
[Fig.~\ref{fig:forecast}(b)]. The point classifier essentially never recovers the rare population (mean
rare-state recall \num{0.0151}); the inductive operator raises it to \num{0.3322}, a \num{0.3171} absolute
gain, recovering the critical regime on the device (\num{0.3968}), shot-noise (\num{0.5706}), and
Hamiltonian (\num{0.3616}) splits. The directional mechanism is most visible on the schedule split, where
train and test are separated along the control parameter: there the directed operator transports
low-$s$ labels forward across the transition to raise accuracy by \num{0.1749} over the point classifier
and by \num{0.0850} over symmetric label propagation, the largest accuracy margin of any split. Rare-state
recall on the schedule split remains \num{0.0000} for every method: the rare critical states lie entirely
beyond the labeled training range of the control parameter, so no diffusion can manufacture a class that
was never reachable from the annotated set (Corollary~\ref{cor:nonident})---we report this null as is. The
directional contribution to critical-regime recovery is isolated by the transductive operator, which holds
the transductive/inductive setting fixed against symmetric propagation and nearly doubles its rare-state
recall (\num{0.4418} versus \num{0.2383} mean; device \num{0.5925} versus \num{0.1520}).

\subsection{Conformal calibration gives distribution-free coverage and removes the ECE cost}
The row-normalized band-limited field yields under-dispersed (over-smoothed) phase probabilities whose
confidence understates the model's accuracy: the raw inductive classifier has an expected calibration error
of \num{0.1374}, and the temperature that minimizes calibration negative log-likelihood is
$T=\num{0.55}<1$---the probabilities must be \emph{sharpened}, not softened. A split-conformal layer and
this temperature scaling, fit on a held-out calibration slice of the annotated states, repair the
miscalibration with a distribution-free guarantee (Fig.~\ref{fig:calibration}, Theorem~\ref{thm:conformal}).
On an exchangeable hold-out the conformal prediction sets attain a realized marginal coverage of
\num{0.8968} against the target $1-\delta=\num{0.90}$, with rare-regime coverage \num{0.9307} and an
average set size of \num{1.81} phases out of four---so the guarantee is met without collapsing to
uninformative sets. Temperature scaling cuts the expected calibration error from \num{0.1374} to
\num{0.0369}, a \SI{73}{\percent} reduction, turning the calibration penalty of graph smoothing into a
calibration guarantee---a prerequisite for trusting a phase label estimated from finitely many shots. We
stress that the coverage guarantee holds under exchangeability of calibration and test states; under the
grouping-transfer splits, where the held-out group induces a distribution shift, coverage is only
approximate, and we report the exchangeable-hold-out figure as the regime in which the theorem applies.
Restoring an exact guarantee under such shift would require a weighted conformal scheme with a known or
estimated likelihood ratio between calibration and test covariates \cite{tibshirani2019covariateshift},
which we leave to future work.

\section{Discussion and conclusion}
\label{sec:discussion}
We introduced a spectral-truncated, drive-directed graph operator for classifying quantum phases across a
control-parameter sweep, and within a controlled synthetic benchmark it outperforms the symmetric smoother
on the dimensions that matter. The directional, non-self-adjoint propagator transports labels along the
sweep, recovering a rare critical regime that a matched point classifier almost entirely misses and,
uniquely, rescuing forward extrapolation across the transition. The spectral truncation band-limits the
operator into a denoised, closed-form solve whose leading modes are provably the optimal low-pass
approximation. The out-of-sample Nystr\"om extension makes the comparison inductive: the method beats the
point classifier on every transfer protocol without ever seeing held-out connectivity, so the accuracy gain
is not the transductive information advantage that inflates
symmetric-smoothing studies---it is the matched inductive control those studies identify as their decisive
missing experiment, and it is what lets the trained operator transfer to states from an unseen device. The
conformal layer converts the calibration penalty of graph smoothing into a distribution-free coverage
guarantee, and the degree-$0$ persistence barcode replaces a passive connected-component count with an
informative, scale-aware topology signal that guides active recovery of the critical regime.

These results connect the long-standing intuition of graph-based semi-supervised learning
\cite{zhou2004local,zhu2002labelprop} to the machine-learning-for-quantum-physics program of recognizing
phases from measurement data \cite{carrasquilla2017ml,carleo2019rmp,biamonte2017qml}; the methodological
advance is to build the classifier from a spectrally truncated, drive-directed operator, in the spirit of
$C^*$-algebraic spectral-truncation kernel machines
\cite{hashimoto2026spectral,vansuijlekom2021spectral,connes2021truncations}, and to make it inductive and
conformally calibrated. The relation to that line of work is sharp: we instantiate spectral truncation and
operator noncommutativity on the state graph and supply a positive-definite noncommutative Gram kernel
(Theorem~\ref{thm:kernel}), but we do \emph{not} develop a new vector-valued reproducing-kernel learning
framework or a variational quantum model as that broader program does
\cite{havlicek2019,schuld2019,patti2022multibasis,cerezo2021vqa}. Our contribution lies along an orthogonal
axis---a directed (non-self-adjoint) transport operator made inductive and conformally calibrated for
quantum-phase classification---and the kernel $\mathcal K$ enters as the structural Gram object rather than
as a learned reproducing-kernel Hilbert space (RKHS) or a parameterized circuit. A directed, truncated, and
inductive graph operator thus offers a principled and falsifiable arena for classifying quantum phases from
measurement data, whose decisive test is validation on classical shadows from a programmable quantum
simulator. The limitations below bound every claim above.

\subsection{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}
The following limitations bound every claim above.
(1) \emph{Synthetic data.} All numbers come from a generated benchmark that emulates shadow-feature
geometry, not measurements from a quantum device; the magnitudes need not transfer to real classical-shadow
data, where shot noise, finite sampling, and device drift are far more severe.
(2) \emph{The transductive operator trades some accuracy.} Held fixed in the transductive setting, the
directed operator nearly doubles symmetric label propagation's rare-state recall but loses a few points of
mean accuracy; it is the inductive variant that recovers and exceeds the baseline's accuracy while removing
the confound, and it is that variant we put forward as the method.
(3) \emph{Calibration is repaired post hoc, under exchangeability.} The conformal guarantee holds for
exchangeable calibration and test states; under grouping-transfer distribution shift (a genuinely new
device or Hamiltonian), realized coverage is only approximate.
(4) \emph{Directionality requires a control-parameter estimate.} The operator consumes a control-parameter
coordinate; on real data this is the experimentally set drive, which is reliable, but an unparameterized or
mislabeled sweep would weaken or mislead the directional transport.
(5) \emph{Standard degree-$0$ topology.} We use the exact $H_0$ persistence barcode---standard persistent
homology \cite{edelsbrunner2010computational,carlsson2009topology} applied here as a multiscale,
scale-aware replacement for the connected-component count, not a new topological construction---and not
higher-order ($H_1$, $H_2$) persistence; the truncation-commutator noncommutativity and the Gram kernel
$\mathcal K$ are finite-rank, not a full $C^*$-algebraic construction.
(6) \emph{The directional prior is aligned with the generator.} The forward kernel encodes the same arrow
of the control parameter that the synthetic sweep is built around, so part of the forward-extrapolation
gain reflects a correctly specified prior; on real data with a noisier or higher-dimensional drive the
advantage may be smaller. The fixed-kernel transport is also not learned: a learned directed propagator and
an RKHS or quantum-kernel use of $\mathcal K$ are left to future work.
(7) \emph{Single benchmark family.} One synthetic generator, however controlled, cannot substitute for the
diversity of real quantum systems; external validation remains the decisive test. The natural next step is
to repeat exactly this comparison on classical shadows from a programmable quantum simulator and on
perturbed-Hamiltonian families, for which the repository provides honest, unexercised ingest stubs.

\section{Theory of spectral-truncated noncommutative graph operators}
\label{sec:theory}
We now make precise, and prove in full, the structural properties the classifier relies upon: that the
symmetric backbone is positive semidefinite and that its leading modes are the optimal band-limit
(Lemma~\ref{lem:dirichlet}, Theorem~\ref{thm:trunc}); that the directed propagator is genuinely non-normal
and that spectral truncation breaks the commutativity of multiplication (Theorems~\ref{thm:noncomm} and
\ref{thm:kernel}); that the truncated directed diffusion is well posed with a closed form that the code
solves directly (Theorem~\ref{thm:fixedpoint}); that the Nystr\"om extension is inductive and consistent
(Theorem~\ref{thm:inductive}); and that the conformal layer has distribution-free coverage
(Theorem~\ref{thm:conformal}). Throughout, $\mathbf{1}$ is the all-ones vector,
$\|\cdot\|_2,\|\cdot\|_F$ the Euclidean and Frobenius norms, $\operatorname{Tr}$ the trace, and
$X\succeq0$ ($X\succ0$) positive (semi)definiteness.

\begin{definition}[State graph, directed propagator, graph-Fourier basis, truncation]
\label{def:graph}
Let $A=A^\top\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times n}$ be the symmetric adjacency of the $k$NN state graph ($A_{ij}\ge0$,
$A_{ii}=0$), $d_i=\sum_j A_{ij}$ the degree, $D=\operatorname{diag}(d_i)$, and $L=D-A$ the combinatorial
Laplacian. Write $S=D^{-1/2}AD^{-1/2}$ for the symmetric normalized adjacency. For a control-parameter
vector $s\in\mathbb{R}^n$, a directional strength $\beta\ge0$, and a scale $\tau>0$, the directed
propagator is $P=D_W^{-1}W$ where $W_{ij}=A_{ij}\,\kappa(s_j-s_i)$,
$\kappa(\delta)=\exp(\beta\tanh(\delta/\tau))$, and $(D_W)_{ii}=\sum_j W_{ij}$; $P$ is row-stochastic. Let
$U_r=[u_1\cdots u_r]\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times r}$ collect the eigenvectors of $S$ for its $r$ largest
eigenvalues (equivalently the $r$ smoothest, lowest-frequency modes of the normalized Laplacian
$\mathcal L=I-S$), $\Pi_r=U_rU_r^\top$ the band-limiting projector,
$\widetilde P=(1-\varepsilon)I+\varepsilon P$ the lazy directed walk for laziness $\varepsilon\in(0,1]$,
and $B=U_r^\top\widetilde P\,U_r=U_r^\top\!\big((1-\varepsilon)I+\varepsilon P\big)U_r$ the truncated lazy
directed transport---the $r\times r$ coordinate representation of the compression $\Pi_r\widetilde P\,\Pi_r$
on the band-limited subspace.
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}[Dirichlet energy and positive semidefiniteness]
\label{lem:dirichlet}
For every $f\in\mathbb{R}^{n}$, $f^\top L f=\tfrac12\sum_{i,j} A_{ij}(f_i-f_j)^2\ge0$, so $L\succeq0$; and
$S=I-D^{-1/2}LD^{-1/2}$ has every eigenvalue in $[-1,1]$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
Both claims reduce to completing the square. Expanding $f^\top Lf=\sum_i d_i f_i^2-\sum_{i,j}A_{ij}f_if_j$
and symmetrizing the diagonal term with $A_{ij}=A_{ji}$ gives
$f^\top Lf=\tfrac12\sum_{i,j}A_{ij}(f_i-f_j)^2\ge0$ since $A_{ij}\ge0$, so $L\succeq0$. The normalized
Laplacian $\mathcal L=D^{-1/2}LD^{-1/2}$ is congruent to $L$ through the invertible $D^{-1/2}$, hence
$\mathcal L\succeq0$ and $S=I-\mathcal L\preceq I$, giving $\lambda(S)\le1$. The signless identity
$f^\top(D+A)f=\tfrac12\sum_{i,j}A_{ij}(f_i+f_j)^2\ge0$ gives $D+A\succeq0$, so
$I+S=D^{-1/2}(D+A)D^{-1/2}\succeq0$ and $\lambda(S)\ge-1$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Spectral truncation is the optimal band-limit]
\label{thm:trunc}
Among all rank-$r$ orthogonal projectors $\Pi$, the band-limiting projector $\Pi_r=U_rU_r^\top$ of
Definition~\ref{def:graph} maximizes the retained smoothing energy $\operatorname{Tr}(\Pi S)$. Moreover,
for any positive-semidefinite graph low-pass filter $R=g(S)$ with $g$ nondecreasing on $[-1,1]$---in
particular the diffusion resolvent $R=(I-\alpha S)^{-1}\succ0$ and the heat kernel $R=e^{-t(I-S)}\succeq0$
that the method's smoothing realizes---$\Pi_r$ furnishes the Eckart--Young--Mirsky-optimal rank-$r$
approximation of $R$: it minimizes $\|R-\Pi R\Pi\|$ over all rank-$r$ orthogonal projectors in every
unitarily invariant norm, with spectral-norm error $g(\lambda_{r+1})$. (For the indefinite $S$ itself,
low-pass truncation is optimal among band-limited projectors but is not in general the unconstrained best
rank-$r$ approximation, which would retain the largest-magnitude rather than the largest-value
eigenvalues; the low-pass choice is the correct one because the classifier applies the monotone PSD filter
$R$, not $S$.)
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
We treat the two claims in turn---the energy claim by a Ky Fan trace argument, the filter claim by
Eckart--Young--Mirsky. Write $S=\sum_j\lambda_j u_ju_j^\top$ with $\lambda_1\ge\cdots\ge\lambda_n$. For a
rank-$r$ projector $\Pi$, $\operatorname{Tr}(\Pi S)=\sum_j\lambda_j\,u_j^\top\Pi u_j$ with
$0\le u_j^\top\Pi u_j\le1$ and $\sum_j u_j^\top\Pi u_j=\operatorname{Tr}\Pi=r$; this linear objective over
the polytope $\{0\le c_j\le1,\sum_j c_j=r\}$ is maximized by putting unit weight on the $r$ largest
$\lambda_j$, i.e., $\Pi=\Pi_r$ (Ky Fan). For $R=g(S)=\sum_j g(\lambda_j)u_ju_j^\top$ with $g$
nondecreasing, the eigenvalues $g(\lambda_j)\ge0$ are ordered with the $\lambda_j$, so the $r$ largest
belong to $u_1,\dots,u_r$; Eckart--Young--Mirsky then gives
$\Pi_rR\Pi_r=\sum_{j\le r}g(\lambda_j)u_ju_j^\top$ as the best rank-$r$ approximation of the
positive-semidefinite $R$ in every unitarily invariant norm, with $\|R-\Pi_rR\Pi_r\|_2=g(\lambda_{r+1})$.
The resolvent $g(\lambda)=1/(1-\alpha\lambda)$ and heat kernel $g(\lambda)=e^{-t(1-\lambda)}$ are
nondecreasing and positive on $[-1,1]$ for $\alpha\in(0,1),\,t>0$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:fixedpoint} gives
$I-\alpha S\succ0$), so the truncated diffusion the method runs is optimally band-limited.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Noncommutativity of the directed propagator and of spectral truncation]
\label{thm:noncomm}
(i) The directed propagator $P$ is non-normal whenever its outgoing and incoming squared transition mass
differ at some state: the commutator diagonal is $[P,P^\top]_{ii}=\sum_j P_{ij}^2-\sum_j P_{ji}^2$, so
$[P,P^\top]\neq0$, and hence $\nu(P)=\|[P,P^\top]\|_F/\|P\|_F^2>0$, as soon as $\sum_j P_{ij}^2\neq\sum_j
P_{ji}^2$ for some $i$; the forward kernel makes the incoming and outgoing reweighting at a state with a
net control-parameter gradient asymmetric, so this holds and $\nu(P)$ strictly exceeds the undirected
control $\nu(P_0)$.
(ii) Spectral truncation breaks the commutativity of pointwise multiplication: for state-wise functions
$\phi,\psi$ the truncated multiplication operators $M_\phi^{(r)}=U_r^\top\!\operatorname{diag}(\phi)U_r$
satisfy
\begin{widetext}
\begin{equation}
[M_\phi^{(r)},M_\psi^{(r)}]=U_r^\top\operatorname{diag}(\phi)(\Pi_r-I)\operatorname{diag}(\psi)U_r
-U_r^\top\operatorname{diag}(\psi)(\Pi_r-I)\operatorname{diag}(\phi)U_r\neq0
\label{eq:commutator}
\end{equation}
\end{widetext}
in general, with the operator-norm bound $\|[M_\phi^{(r)},M_\psi^{(r)}]\|_2\le 2\|\phi\|_\infty
\|\psi\|_\infty$ (the Frobenius norm can grow as $\sqrt{r}$ across the band), vanishing only in the
full-rank limit $\Pi_r=I$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Both parts are direct computations on the relevant operator products. (i) The diagonal entries of the two
products are $(PP^\top)_{ii}=\sum_j P_{ij}^2$ (the squared outgoing transition mass at $i$) and
$(P^\top P)_{ii}=\sum_j P_{ji}^2$ (the squared incoming mass), so $[P,P^\top]_{ii}=\sum_j(P_{ij}^2-P_{ji}^2)$.
If $P$ were normal the commutator would vanish, forcing $\sum_j P_{ij}^2=\sum_j P_{ji}^2$ at every state.
Row-stochasticity fixes the row sums $\sum_j P_{ij}=1$ but constrains neither the column sums nor the
squared masses; the directional reweighting $W_{ij}=A_{ij}\exp(\beta\tanh((s_j-s_i)/\tau))$ up-weights
forward edges and down-weights backward ones, so a state with a net forward (or backward) control-parameter
gradient has unequal incoming and outgoing squared mass and a nonzero commutator diagonal. Hence
$[P,P^\top]\neq0$ and $\nu(P)>0$; the undirected control $\beta=0$ retains only the residual
degree-normalization asymmetry of $D_W^{-1}W$, giving the smaller but still positive $\nu(P_0)$ reported in
Table~\ref{tab:extended}.
(ii) Writing $\operatorname{diag}(\phi)\operatorname{diag}(\psi)=\operatorname{diag}(\phi\psi)
=\operatorname{diag}(\psi)\operatorname{diag}(\phi)$ and inserting $I=\Pi_r+(I-\Pi_r)$ between the two
diagonal factors,
\begin{widetext}
\begin{equation}
M_\phi^{(r)}M_\psi^{(r)}=U_r^\top\operatorname{diag}(\phi)\Pi_r\operatorname{diag}(\psi)U_r
=U_r^\top\operatorname{diag}(\phi\psi)U_r-U_r^\top\operatorname{diag}(\phi)(I-\Pi_r)\operatorname{diag}(\psi)U_r,
\end{equation}
\end{widetext}
and symmetrically for $M_\psi^{(r)}M_\phi^{(r)}$. Subtracting gives the commutator~\eqref{eq:commutator},
whose two terms cancel only when $(I-\Pi_r)\operatorname{diag}(\psi)U_r$ and
$(I-\Pi_r)\operatorname{diag}(\phi)U_r$ are aligned---generically false for $r<n$. The bound follows from
$\|U_r\|_2=1$, $\|\operatorname{diag}(\phi)\|_2=\|\phi\|_\infty$, and $\|I-\Pi_r\|_2=1$; at $r=n$,
$\Pi_r=I$ and the commutator is $U^\top[\operatorname{diag}(\phi),\operatorname{diag}(\psi)]U=0$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[A positive-definite noncommutative truncated operator kernel]
\label{thm:kernel}
For the shadow-feature channels $\phi_1,\dots,\phi_d$ (the columns of $X$) with truncated multiplication
operators $M_c=M_{\phi_c}^{(r)}=U_r^\top\operatorname{diag}(\phi_c)U_r$, the truncated operator kernel
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:nckernel}
\mathcal K=\sum_{c=1}^{d} M_c M_c^\top
\end{equation}
is positive semidefinite, $\mathcal K\succeq0$, and positive definite whenever the truncated
multiplications share no common left-null vector. Its generators $M_c$ do not pairwise commute
[Theorem~\ref{thm:noncomm}(ii)], so $\mathcal K$ is the symmetric Gram object of a genuinely noncommutative
truncated operator $*$-algebra on the state graph---the graph instance of the noncommutative operator
structure underlying spectral-truncation kernel machines \cite{hashimoto2026spectral}. At the reported
scale $\lambda_{\min}(\mathcal K)=\num{1.62}>0$ (Table~\ref{tab:extended}), so $\mathcal K\succ0$ is
strictly positive definite.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
For any $v$, $v^\top\mathcal K v=\sum_c v^\top M_cM_c^\top v=\sum_c\|M_c^\top v\|_2^2\ge0$, so
$\mathcal K\succeq0$ as a sum of Gram terms. Equality forces $M_c^\top v=0$ for every $c$, i.e., $v$ is a
common left-null vector of the truncated multiplications; excluding this (which the strictly positive
$\lambda_{\min}(\mathcal K)$ confirms) gives $\mathcal K\succ0$. The noncommutativity of the generators is
Theorem~\ref{thm:noncomm}(ii). Because $\mathcal K$ is symmetric positive (semi)definite it defines a valid
kernel on the band-limited subspace whose off-diagonal structure is governed by the non-vanishing
commutators $[M_b,M_c]$, which is what distinguishes it from a separable (commutative) construction.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Well-posedness and closed form of the truncated directed diffusion]
\label{thm:fixedpoint}
Fix $\alpha\in(0,1)$ and write $\rho=\rho(\alpha B)$ for the spectral radius of the truncated transport
$\alpha B$. If $\rho<1$ then $I-\alpha B$ is invertible, the reduced normal equations
$(I-\alpha B)\,c=(1-\alpha)U_r^\top F_0$ have the unique solution
$c^\star=(1-\alpha)(I-\alpha B)^{-1}U_r^\top F_0$, the lifted field $F^\star=U_rc^\star$ is the limit of
the band-limited power iteration $c^{(m+1)}=\alpha Bc^{(m)}+(1-\alpha)U_r^\top F_0$ from any start, and the
error contracts as $\rho^{m}$ up to a constant. A simple sufficient condition is
$\alpha\,\sigma_{\max}(B)<1$ with $\sigma_{\max}(B)\le(1-\varepsilon)+\varepsilon\,\sigma_{\max}(P)$; this
bound is conservative because $P$ is row-stochastic with $\sigma_{\max}(P)$ possibly exceeding $1$, and the
operative quantity is the spectral radius, which at the reported configuration is $\rho=\num{0.9209}<1$
(Table~\ref{tab:extended}, integrity gate \texttt{diffusion\_well\_posed}). Moreover $F^\star$ is invariant
to the sign gauge of $U_r$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
The statement is the contraction-mapping theorem specialized to the Neumann series of $\alpha B$. If
$\rho(\alpha B)<1$ then $\alpha B$ has no eigenvalue equal to $1$, so $I-\alpha B$ is nonsingular and the
Neumann series $\sum_{m\ge0}(\alpha B)^m=(I-\alpha B)^{-1}$ converges (its terms decay geometrically once
$\|(\alpha B)^m\|^{1/m}\to\rho<1$ by Gelfand's formula). Subtracting the fixed-point relation
$c^\star=\alpha Bc^\star+(1-\alpha)U_r^\top F_0$ from the iteration gives
$c^{(m+1)}-c^\star=\alpha B(c^{(m)}-c^\star)$, so $c^{(m)}-c^\star=(\alpha B)^m(c^{(0)}-c^\star)\to0$ at the
asymptotic rate $\rho$. For the sufficient condition, $\rho(\alpha B)\le\alpha\,\sigma_{\max}(B)$ (spectral
radius bounded by spectral norm), and $\sigma_{\max}(B)\le(1-\varepsilon)+\varepsilon\,
\sigma_{\max}(U_r^\top PU_r)\le(1-\varepsilon)+\varepsilon\,\sigma_{\max}(P)$ by the triangle inequality and
the fact that a compression by the orthonormal $U_r$ cannot increase the largest singular value. The
closed-form solve the code runs requires only $\rho<1$, which the integrity gate verifies. Finally,
replacing $U_r$ by $U_rG$ for a diagonal sign matrix $G=G^{-1}$ sends $B\mapsto GBG$ and
$U_r^\top F_0\mapsto GU_r^\top F_0$, whence $c^\star\mapsto Gc^\star$ and
$F^\star=U_rc^\star\mapsto U_rGGc^\star=U_rc^\star$ is unchanged.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Non-identifiability of a label-free region]
\label{cor:nonident}
If a phase is absent from the labeled seed $F_0$ on every state reachable (in the band-limited field) from
the test region, the diffusion assigns it zero mass there and cannot recover it. In particular, when the
held-out test set lies beyond the labeled support along the control parameter---as on the schedule split---%
rare critical states confined to that region are unrecoverable by smoothing alone, which is the mechanism
behind the null schedule-split rare-state recall.
\end{corollary}
\begin{proof}
$F^\star=(1-\alpha)U_r(I-\alpha B)^{-1}U_r^\top F_0$ is linear in $F_0$; if the $c$-th column of $F_0$ is
zero on the labeled set then that column of $U_r^\top F_0$ is the projection of a vector supported off the
reachable region, and the band-limited propagation, whose support is contained in the closure of the
labeled region under the directed transport, leaves the test rows of that column at zero. The argmax
read-out can then never select the absent phase on those rows.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Inductive consistency of the Nystr\"om extension]
\label{thm:inductive}
Let $F^\star_{\mathrm{tr}}$ be the band-limited field on the training states and, for a held-out state
$x_\ast$, let $\widehat F(x_\ast)=\sum_{i\in\mathcal N(x_\ast)} w_i\,F^\star_{\mathrm{tr}}(x_i)$ be its
Gaussian-weighted average over the $k$ nearest \emph{training} states, $\sum_i w_i=1$. The estimator uses no
test--test edge and no held-out connectivity, so the classifier is inductive. If the field is $L$-Lipschitz
on the data manifold, then $\|\widehat F(x_\ast)-F^\star(x_\ast)\|_2\le
L\max_{i\in\mathcal N(x_\ast)}\|x_i-x_\ast\|_2$, which tends to $0$ as the training sample becomes dense;
hence $\widehat F\to F^\star$ pointwise under standard manifold-density conditions.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
The estimator reads only the training field and the test-to-train affinities; it never forms an edge
between two test states nor consults the held-out adjacency, so by construction inference on $x_\ast$ is
independent of all other test states---the definition of an inductive predictor. For the bound, convexity of
the weighted average and the triangle inequality give
\begin{widetext}
\begin{equation}
\|\widehat F(x_\ast)-F^\star(x_\ast)\|_2=\Big\|\textstyle\sum_i w_i\big(F^\star(x_i)-F^\star(x_\ast)\big)\Big\|_2
\le\sum_i w_i\|F^\star(x_i)-F^\star(x_\ast)\|_2\le L\sum_i w_i\|x_i-x_\ast\|_2\le L\max_i\|x_i-x_\ast\|_2.
\end{equation}
\end{widetext}
As the sample density increases the $k$NN radius $\max_i\|x_i-x_\ast\|_2\to0$ almost surely under standard
conditions, so the right-hand side vanishes.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Distribution-free conformal coverage]
\label{thm:conformal}
Let the nonconformity score of a state with predicted probabilities $p$ and true phase $y$ be $s=1-p_y$,
computed by the fitted classifier. Given $n_{\mathrm{cal}}$ calibration states exchangeable with a test
state, let $\hat q$ be the $\lceil(n_{\mathrm{cal}}+1)(1-\delta)\rceil/n_{\mathrm{cal}}$ empirical quantile
of the calibration scores and form the prediction set $C(x)=\{c:1-p_c(x)\le\hat q\}$. Then
$\Pr[y_{\mathrm{test}}\in C(x_{\mathrm{test}})]\ge1-\delta$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
By exchangeability of the $n_{\mathrm{cal}}+1$ scores
$\{s_1,\dots,s_{n_{\mathrm{cal}}},s_{\mathrm{test}}\}$, the rank of $s_{\mathrm{test}}$ among them is
uniform on $\{1,\dots,n_{\mathrm{cal}}+1\}$, so $\Pr[s_{\mathrm{test}}\le\hat q]\ge
\lceil(n_{\mathrm{cal}}+1)(1-\delta)\rceil/(n_{\mathrm{cal}}+1)\ge1-\delta$, where $\hat q$ is the
$\lceil(n_{\mathrm{cal}}+1)(1-\delta)\rceil$-th smallest calibration score. Since
$y_{\mathrm{test}}\in C(x_{\mathrm{test}})\iff s_{\mathrm{test}}=1-p_{y_{\mathrm{test}}}\le\hat q$, the
event has probability at least $1-\delta$.
\end{proof}

\section{Inductive classification: generalization and design theory}
\label{sec:inductive-theory}
The results of Appendix~\ref{sec:theory} establish that the operator is well posed, optimally band-limited,
noncommutative, inductive, and conformally covered. They do not yet answer the four statistical questions
that decide whether the classifier is trustworthy when it is used as intended: to \emph{extrapolate} a phase
label to control-parameter values that were never labeled. We treat those four questions here, under one
statistical reading of the method. Classifying the phase of a state from its shadow features is a
classification problem; the control-parameter sweep is a designed experiment whose annotated slice induces a
source distribution and whose held-out slice induces a shifted target distribution; the truncated directed
operator is a structured probabilistic surrogate for the phase field over the directed sweep manifold; and
choosing which control points to prepare and measure is an experimental-design problem. Under this reading we
prove, in order: (\S\ref{ssub:covshift}) an importance-weighted, integral-probability-metric generalization
bound for inductive classification under the covariate shift the sweep induces, with an explicit condition
for reliable forward extrapolation that sharpens the non-identifiability of
Corollary~\ref{cor:nonident}; (\S\ref{ssub:estconsistency}) consistency of the estimated directed
spectral-truncated operator---its band-limited eigenspace, its truncated transport $B$, and its field
$F^\star$ all converge to their population versions---via a Davis--Kahan $\sin\Theta$ bound driven by matrix
concentration; (\S\ref{ssub:truncest}) a decomposition of the total error into a spectral-truncation tail and
an estimation term, with the resulting bias--variance trade-off in the truncation rank $r$ made explicit and
its minimizer characterized, formalizing the interior operating point of Fig.~\ref{fig:operator}(c); and
(\S\ref{ssub:design}) two guarantees for designing the sweep---a $(1-1/e)$ greedy-submodularity guarantee for
selecting control points that maximize information gain about the field, and consistency of a change-point
estimator that locates the phase boundary $s_0$ from the swept classifier score. Throughout we reuse the
notation of Definition~\ref{def:graph}; new symbols are defined where introduced.

\subsection{Inductive generalization under covariate shift along the sweep}
\label{ssub:covshift}
We formalize the inductive task of Theorem~\ref{thm:inductive} as classification under distribution shift.
Write $z=(x,s)\in\mathcal Z$ for a state's shadow-feature vector $x$ together with its control-parameter
coordinate $s$, and $y\in\{1,\dots,C\}$ for its phase. The trained field induces, through the Nystr\"om
read-out of Theorem~\ref{thm:inductive} and the argmax over $C$ phases, a classifier
$h:\mathcal Z\to\{1,\dots,C\}$ drawn from the hypothesis class $\mathcal H$ of all such read-outs obtainable
from the band-limited field. Let $\ell(h(z),y)\in[0,1]$ be the $0/1$ loss.

\begin{definition}[Source and target of the sweep, density ratio, discrepancy]
\label{def:distributions}
The annotated training slice induces a \emph{source} distribution $\mathcal P$ on $\mathcal Z\times\mathcal
Y$; the held-out slice at unseen control parameters induces a shifted \emph{target} distribution $\mathcal
Q$. We assume \emph{covariate shift}: the phase rule is stable across the sweep, $\mathcal
P(y\mid z)=\mathcal Q(y\mid z)$, while the marginals differ, $\mathcal P(z)\neq\mathcal Q(z)$. Write
$R(h;\mathcal D)=\mathbb E_{(z,y)\sim\mathcal D}\,\ell(h(z),y)$ for the risk under $\mathcal D$, and
$\widehat R_{\mathrm P}(h)=\tfrac1m\sum_{i=1}^m\ell(h(z_i),y_i)$ for the empirical source risk over the $m$
annotated states $\{(z_i,y_i)\}\sim\mathcal P$. When $\mathcal Q(z)\ll\mathcal P(z)$ let
$w(z)=\mathrm d\mathcal Q/\mathrm d\mathcal P(z)$ be the covariate density ratio, assumed bounded,
$0\le w(z)\le B_w<\infty$, with second moment $\mathbb E_{\mathcal P}[w^2]=:\|w\|_{2,\mathcal P}^2$. For a
class $\mathcal G$ of test functions on $\mathcal Z$, the integral probability metric (IPM) is
$d_{\mathcal G}(\mathcal P,\mathcal Q)=\sup_{g\in\mathcal G}\big|\mathbb E_{\mathcal P}g-\mathbb
E_{\mathcal Q}g\big|$ \cite{sriperumbudur2012empirical}.
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}[Importance-weighting is unbiased and concentrates]
\label{lem:iw}
Under Definition~\ref{def:distributions} the reweighted source risk equals the target risk,
$R(h;\mathcal Q)=\mathbb E_{\mathcal P}\!\big[w(z)\,\ell(h(z),y)\big]$, so the plug-in estimator
$\widehat R_{w}(h)=\tfrac1m\sum_{i=1}^m w(z_i)\,\ell(h(z_i),y_i)$ is unbiased for $R(h;\mathcal Q)$. For any
fixed $h$, with probability at least $1-\eta$ over the $m$ source draws,
\begin{equation}
\big|\widehat R_{w}(h)-R(h;\mathcal Q)\big|\le B_w\sqrt{\frac{\log(2/\eta)}{2m}} .
\label{eq:iw-hoeffding}
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
By covariate shift $\mathcal Q(y\mid z)=\mathcal P(y\mid z)$, so for any integrable $\varphi$,
$\mathbb E_{\mathcal Q}[\varphi(z,y)]=\mathbb E_{\mathcal Q(z)}\mathbb E_{\mathcal Q(y\mid z)}[\varphi]
=\mathbb E_{\mathcal P(z)}\big[w(z)\,\mathbb E_{\mathcal P(y\mid z)}\varphi\big]
=\mathbb E_{\mathcal P}[w(z)\varphi(z,y)]$, the change-of-measure identity; taking $\varphi=\ell(h(z),y)$
gives unbiasedness. Each summand $w(z_i)\ell(h(z_i),y_i)$ lies in $[0,B_w]$ because
$\ell\in[0,1]$ and $0\le w\le B_w$; the terms are i.i.d.\ with mean $R(h;\mathcal Q)$, so Hoeffding's
inequality for bounded independent variables gives~\eqref{eq:iw-hoeffding}.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Covariate-shift generalization bound for inductive phase classification]
\label{thm:covshift}
Let $\mathcal H$ be a hypothesis class with the property that the induced loss class $\ell\circ\mathcal
H=\{z\mapsto\ell(h(z),y):h\in\mathcal H\}$ has empirical Rademacher complexity
$\widehat{\mathfrak R}_m(\ell\circ\mathcal H)$ on the $m$ annotated source states. Then with probability at
least $1-\eta$ over the source sample, simultaneously for every $h\in\mathcal H$,
\begin{widetext}
\begin{equation}
\underbrace{R(h;\mathcal Q)}_{\text{target (extrapolation) risk}}\ \le\
\underbrace{\widehat R_{\mathrm P}(h)}_{\text{empirical source risk}}
\ +\ \underbrace{d_{\ell\circ\mathcal H}(\mathcal P,\mathcal Q)}_{\text{sweep shift (IPM)}}
\ +\ \underbrace{2\,\widehat{\mathfrak R}_m(\ell\circ\mathcal H)+3\sqrt{\tfrac{\log(4/\eta)}{2m}}}_{\text{estimation}} ,
\label{eq:covshift-ipm}
\end{equation}
\end{widetext}
and, on the tighter importance-weighted path, with probability at least $1-\eta$ for every $h\in\mathcal H$,
\begin{equation}
R(h;\mathcal Q)\ \le\ \widehat R_{w}(h)\ +\ 2B_w\,\widehat{\mathfrak R}_m(\ell\circ\mathcal H)
\ +\ 3B_w\sqrt{\tfrac{\log(4/\eta)}{2m}} .
\label{eq:covshift-iw}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
We prove the two displays separately; each is a standard uniform-convergence argument adapted to the shift.

\emph{IPM path~\eqref{eq:covshift-ipm}.} Fix $h$. Add and subtract the two source risks:
\begin{equation}
R(h;\mathcal Q)=\underbrace{\big(R(h;\mathcal Q)-R(h;\mathcal P)\big)}_{(\mathrm I)}
+\underbrace{\big(R(h;\mathcal P)-\widehat R_{\mathrm P}(h)\big)}_{(\mathrm{II})}
+\widehat R_{\mathrm P}(h).
\end{equation}
For $(\mathrm I)$, the map $z\mapsto\mathbb E[\ell(h(z),y)\mid z]$ belongs to $\ell\circ\mathcal H$ (the
conditional risk is a member of the loss class because $\mathcal Q(y\mid z)=\mathcal P(y\mid z)$ makes it the
same functional under both measures), so by definition of the IPM
$(\mathrm I)=\mathbb E_{\mathcal Q}[\ell]-\mathbb E_{\mathcal P}[\ell]\le d_{\ell\circ\mathcal
H}(\mathcal P,\mathcal Q)$. For $(\mathrm{II})$, the standard symmetrization bound for bounded losses gives,
uniformly over $\mathcal H$ with probability $\ge1-\eta$, $\sup_{h}(R(h;\mathcal P)-\widehat R_{\mathrm
P}(h))\le 2\widehat{\mathfrak R}_m(\ell\circ\mathcal H)+3\sqrt{\log(4/\eta)/2m}$ (McDiarmid on the
supremum plus the Rademacher bound on its expectation, with the empirical-Rademacher version costing the
second $\sqrt{\cdot}$ term). Combining the three pieces and taking the supremum over $h$ gives
\eqref{eq:covshift-ipm}.

\emph{Importance-weighted path~\eqref{eq:covshift-iw}.} By Lemma~\ref{lem:iw}, $\widehat R_w(h)$ is unbiased
for $R(h;\mathcal Q)$. The reweighted loss class $\{z\mapsto w(z)\ell(h(z),y):h\in\mathcal H\}$ takes values
in $[0,B_w]$; scaling the loss by the fixed multiplier $w(z)\le B_w$ multiplies the Rademacher complexity by
at most $B_w$ (contraction/positive-homogeneity of Rademacher averages under multiplication by a fixed
$[0,B_w]$-bounded function), and rescaling the range from $[0,1]$ to $[0,B_w]$ multiplies the McDiarmid
deviation term by $B_w$. The same symmetrization argument as above, now on the reweighted class, yields
$\sup_h(R(h;\mathcal Q)-\widehat R_w(h))\le 2B_w\widehat{\mathfrak R}_m(\ell\circ\mathcal
H)+3B_w\sqrt{\log(4/\eta)/2m}$ with probability $\ge1-\eta$, which is~\eqref{eq:covshift-iw}.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Reading the bound]
\label{rem:covshift}
Both displays isolate the price of extrapolating across the sweep in a single term. In~\eqref{eq:covshift-ipm}
it is the IPM $d_{\ell\circ\mathcal H}(\mathcal P,\mathcal Q)$---the largest gap, over classifiers, between
source and target loss---which is exactly the $\mathcal H$-divergence of domain-adaptation theory
\cite{bendavid2010theory} specialized to the loss class; a small IPM certifies that no classifier in
$\mathcal H$ can tell the labeled and held-out slices of the sweep apart by its errors, so source accuracy
transfers. In~\eqref{eq:covshift-iw} the price is the density-ratio bound $B_w$ (and $\|w\|_{2,\mathcal P}$
through the variance), inflating the estimation term by exactly how far the target slice reaches into the
tail of the labeled sweep. The forward-schedule protocol of the main text is the regime in which either
quantity is largest, which is why it is the hardest split.
\end{remark}

\begin{corollary}[When forward extrapolation is reliable]
\label{cor:extrap}
Suppose (a) the conditional phase probabilities $z\mapsto \eta_c(z)=\mathcal P(y=c\mid z)$ are $L_\eta$%
-Lipschitz on $\mathcal Z$ and the classifier read-out $h$ is a fixed measurable function of the
$L$-Lipschitz field $F^\star$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:inductive}); and (b) the target slice is reachable, i.e.\
$w(z)\le B_w<\infty$ on $\operatorname{supp}\mathcal Q$ (no target mass outside the source support closure
under the directed transport). Then the IPM obeys $d_{\ell\circ\mathcal H}(\mathcal P,\mathcal Q)\le
(L_\eta+L)\,W_1(\mathcal P_{\!z},\mathcal Q_z)$, where $W_1$ is the $1$-Wasserstein distance between the
control-augmented covariate marginals, and Theorem~\ref{thm:covshift} gives target risk controlled by source
risk plus $O\big((L_\eta+L)\,W_1+\widehat{\mathfrak R}_m\big)$; extrapolation is reliable precisely when the
covariate marginals are close in $W_1$ and the phase rule is smooth. Conversely, if condition~(b) fails---%
the target region has covariate mass outside the reachable support---then $B_w=\infty$, the importance-%
weighted bound is vacuous, and no smoothing can transport a label there, which is
Corollary~\ref{cor:nonident} in statistical form.
\end{corollary}
\begin{proof}
The loss class $\ell\circ\mathcal H$ under $0/1$ loss and a Lipschitz score read-out consists of functions
whose conditional-risk representatives $z\mapsto\sum_c\eta_c(z)\,\mathbf 1[h(z)\neq c]$ are, off a measure-%
zero decision boundary, $(L_\eta+L)$-Lipschitz in $z$ (the $\eta_c$ contribute $L_\eta$ and the read-out's
dependence on the $L$-Lipschitz field contributes $L$). By the Kantorovich--Rubinstein duality, the IPM over
any family of $\Lambda$-Lipschitz functions equals $\Lambda\,W_1$; hence
$d_{\ell\circ\mathcal H}(\mathcal P,\mathcal Q)\le(L_\eta+L)\,W_1(\mathcal P_{\!z},\mathcal Q_z)$.
Substituting into~\eqref{eq:covshift-ipm} gives the stated rate. If instead $\mathcal Q$ places mass where
$\mathcal P$ has none (target beyond the reachable support), the Radon--Nikodym derivative $w$ is unbounded,
so the right-hand side of~\eqref{eq:covshift-iw} is infinite; and by Corollary~\ref{cor:nonident} the
band-limited field is zero for any phase absent from the labeled support reachable under the directed
transport, so the label cannot be produced there by diffusion. Both facts express the same obstruction.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Consistency of the estimated directed spectral-truncated operator}
\label{ssub:estconsistency}
The operator $S$, its band-limited eigenbasis $U_r$, the truncated transport $B$, and the field $F^\star$
are all estimated from a finite, noisy sample of states; we now show they converge to a well-defined
population target as the sample and its measurement fidelity improve. We model the estimation error abstractly:
let $S$ denote the population symmetric normalized adjacency (the ideal, shot-noise-free operator) and
$\widehat S$ its finite-sample, finitely-measured realization, with $E=\widehat S-S$ the estimation error;
$\widehat U_r$ collects the top-$r$ eigenvectors of $\widehat S$, and $\widehat B,\widehat F^\star$ are the
truncated transport and field built from $\widehat U_r$. The population spectral gap at the truncation rank
is $\gamma_r=\lambda_r(S)-\lambda_{r+1}(S)$.

\begin{lemma}[Operator concentration]
\label{lem:conc}
Suppose the estimation error decomposes as a sum of $N$ independent, symmetric, mean-zero perturbations
$E=\sum_{k=1}^N X_k$ (one per independent shot-noise batch or per resampled edge weight) with
$\|X_k\|_2\le b$ almost surely and matrix variance
$\sigma^2=\big\|\sum_k\mathbb E X_k^2\big\|_2$. Then for every $t>0$,
$\Pr\big[\|E\|_2\ge t\big]\le 2n\,\exp\!\big(-t^2/(2\sigma^2+\tfrac23 bt)\big)$, and consequently
$\|E\|_2=\|\widehat S-S\|_2=O_{\mathrm P}\big(\sqrt{\sigma^2\log n}+b\log n\big)$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
This is the matrix Bernstein inequality \cite{tropp2015introduction} for a sum of independent, symmetric,
mean-zero matrices with the stated almost-sure and variance bounds; its tail is
$2n\exp(-t^2/(2\sigma^2+\tfrac23 bt))$. Setting the right-hand side to a constant and solving for $t$ yields
the stochastic order $O_{\mathrm P}(\sqrt{\sigma^2\log n}+b\log n)$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Davis--Kahan consistency of the band-limited subspace and the truncated operator]
\label{thm:estconsistency}
Let $\gamma_r=\lambda_r(S)-\lambda_{r+1}(S)>0$ be the population truncation gap. Then the estimated
band-limited eigenspace obeys the $\sin\Theta$ bound
\begin{equation}
\|\sin\Theta(\widehat U_r,U_r)\|_2\ \le\ \frac{\|\widehat S-S\|_2}{\gamma_r}
\ \le\ \frac{\|E\|_2}{\gamma_r},
\label{eq:davis-kahan}
\end{equation}
so there is an $r\times r$ orthogonal $O$ with $\|\widehat U_rO-U_r\|_2\le\sqrt2\,\|E\|_2/\gamma_r$. If, in
addition, $\|E\|_2\to0$ in probability (e.g.\ at the rate of Lemma~\ref{lem:conc} with
$\sigma^2\log n\to0$), then the projector, the truncated transport, and the field are all consistent:
$\|\widehat\Pi_r-\Pi_r\|_2\le2\|E\|_2/\gamma_r\to0$,
$\|\widehat B-B\|_2\xrightarrow{\ \mathrm P\ }0$ (up to the gauge $O$), and, provided the population
truncated diffusion is well posed ($\rho(\alpha B)<1$, Theorem~\ref{thm:fixedpoint}),
$\|\widehat F^\star-F^\star\|_2\xrightarrow{\ \mathrm P\ }0$. Hence the learned band-limited phase
representation is a consistent estimator of its population version.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Inequality~\eqref{eq:davis-kahan} is the Davis--Kahan $\sin\Theta$ theorem in the Yu--Wang--Samworth
variant \cite{yu2015useful}: with a nonzero gap $\gamma_r$ between the $r$-th and $(r{+}1)$-th population
eigenvalues, the sine of the largest principal angle between the top-$r$ eigenspaces of $S$ and
$\widehat S=S+E$ is at most $\|E\|_2/\gamma_r$; the accompanying rotation bound
$\|\widehat U_rO-U_r\|_2\le\sqrt2\,\|\sin\Theta\|_2$ produces the aligning orthogonal $O$. For the
projector, $\Pi_r=U_rU_r^\top$ is gauge-invariant (independent of $O$), and the standard identity
$\|\widehat\Pi_r-\Pi_r\|_2=\|\sin\Theta(\widehat U_r,U_r)\|_2$ (the projector distance equals the largest
principal sine) upgraded by a factor $2$ for the two-sided bound gives
$\|\widehat\Pi_r-\Pi_r\|_2\le2\|E\|_2/\gamma_r$. For the truncated transport, choose the gauge $O$ and write
$\widehat B=(\widehat U_rO)^\top\widetilde{\widehat P}(\widehat U_rO)$; then
\begin{equation}
\|\widehat B-B\|_2\le 2\|\widehat U_rO-U_r\|_2\,\|\widetilde P\|_2+\|\widetilde{\widehat P}-\widetilde
P\|_2,
\end{equation}
by adding and subtracting $U_r^\top\widetilde{\widehat P}(\widehat U_rO)$ and
$U_r^\top\widetilde{\widehat P}U_r$ and using $\|U_r\|_2=\|\widehat U_r\|_2=1$; both terms vanish in
probability because $\|\widehat U_rO-U_r\|_2\le\sqrt2\|E\|_2/\gamma_r\to0$ and, by the same concentration
applied to the directed weights, $\|\widetilde{\widehat P}-\widetilde P\|_2\to0$. Finally, when
$\rho(\alpha B)<1$ the resolvent $(I-\alpha B)^{-1}$ is a bounded, continuous function of $B$ on a
neighborhood (the map $M\mapsto(I-\alpha M)^{-1}$ is Lipschitz where $I-\alpha M$ is boundedly invertible, by
the first resolvent identity $(I-\alpha\widehat B)^{-1}-(I-\alpha B)^{-1}=\alpha(I-\alpha\widehat
B)^{-1}(\widehat B-B)(I-\alpha B)^{-1}$), so $\widehat F^\star=\widehat U_r(1-\alpha)(I-\alpha\widehat
B)^{-1}\widehat U_r^\top F_0\to F^\star$ in probability by the continuous-mapping theorem, using the
subspace and transport consistency just established and the gauge-invariance of $F^\star$
(Theorem~\ref{thm:fixedpoint}).
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[The gap sets the price of consistency]
\label{rem:gap}
The rate is $\|\widehat U_rO-U_r\|_2=O_{\mathrm P}(\sqrt{\sigma^2\log n}/\gamma_r)$: consistency is fast where
the graph-Fourier spectrum has a clear gap after mode $r$ and slow where it is flat. The smooth decay of the
$S$-spectrum reported in Fig.~\ref{fig:operator}(b) (from $1$ to $\approx0.41$ over the first $80$ modes) is
exactly what makes an interior $r$ with a usable gap available, and it is the same gap that governs the
truncation--estimation trade-off of the next subsection.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Truncation error, estimation error, and the optimal rank}
\label{ssub:truncest}
Theorem~\ref{thm:trunc} showed that, at a \emph{fixed} rank, low-pass truncation is the optimal band-limit;
Theorem~\ref{thm:estconsistency} showed that, at a fixed operator, the estimate is consistent. The choice of
$r$ trades these against each other, and we now bound the total error and characterize its minimizer. Let
$R=g(S)$ be the monotone positive-semidefinite diffusion filter of Theorem~\ref{thm:trunc} (resolvent or
heat kernel), $\widehat R_r=\widehat\Pi_r g(\widehat S)\widehat\Pi_r$ its estimated rank-$r$ truncation, and
measure error in spectral norm on the action of the filter that drives the field.

\begin{theorem}[Bias--variance decomposition of the truncated estimate and optimal $r$]
\label{thm:truncest}
With $g$ nondecreasing and $1$-Lipschitz on $[-1,1]$ (both the resolvent with $\alpha\in(0,1)$ after rescaling
and the heat kernel satisfy a Lipschitz bound $L_g$; absorb $L_g$ into the constant), the total error of the
estimated rank-$r$ diffusion filter splits into a truncation tail and an estimation term,
\begin{widetext}
\begin{equation}
\underbrace{\big\|R-\widehat R_r\big\|_2}_{\text{total}}
\ \le\ \underbrace{g(\lambda_{r+1})}_{\text{truncation bias }b(r)}
\ +\ \underbrace{\frac{c_1\,\|E\|_2}{\gamma_r}}_{\text{estimation variance }v(r)}
\ +\ c_2\,\|E\|_2 ,
\label{eq:truncest}
\end{equation}
\end{widetext}
where $c_1,c_2$ are absolute constants (from Theorem~\ref{thm:estconsistency} and the $1$-Lipschitz $g$) and
$\gamma_r=\lambda_r(S)-\lambda_{r+1}(S)$. The truncation bias $b(r)=g(\lambda_{r+1})$ is nonincreasing in
$r$ (fewer discarded modes, smaller tail), while the estimation term $v(r)=c_1\|E\|_2/\gamma_r$ is large
wherever the spectral gap $\gamma_r$ is small; the optimal rank is
$r^\star\in\arg\min_r\{g(\lambda_{r+1})+c_1\|E\|_2/\gamma_r\}$, which for a spectrum whose gaps shrink with
$r$ is attained at a finite interior $r^\star<n$, not at $r=n$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Insert the population truncation as an intermediary and use the triangle inequality,
\begin{equation}
\|R-\widehat R_r\|_2\le\underbrace{\|R-\Pi_rR\Pi_r\|_2}_{(\mathrm A)}
+\underbrace{\|\Pi_rR\Pi_r-\widehat\Pi_r\widehat R\widehat\Pi_r\|_2}_{(\mathrm B)},
\end{equation}
where $\widehat R=g(\widehat S)$. Term $(\mathrm A)$ is the population truncation error: by
Theorem~\ref{thm:trunc}, for the monotone PSD filter $R=g(S)$ the low-pass projector $\Pi_r$ gives the
Eckart--Young--Mirsky-optimal rank-$r$ approximation with spectral-norm error exactly $g(\lambda_{r+1})$,
so $(\mathrm A)=g(\lambda_{r+1})=b(r)$. Term $(\mathrm B)$ is the estimation error at fixed rank. Expanding
$\Pi_rR\Pi_r-\widehat\Pi_r\widehat R\widehat\Pi_r$ by adding and subtracting
$\widehat\Pi_rR\Pi_r$ and $\widehat\Pi_r\widehat R\Pi_r$ and using $\|\Pi_r\|_2=\|\widehat\Pi_r\|_2=1$ and
$\|R\|_2\le g(1)$ gives
\begin{equation}
(\mathrm B)\le 2\,g(1)\,\|\widehat\Pi_r-\Pi_r\|_2+\|\widehat R-R\|_2 .
\end{equation}
By Theorem~\ref{thm:estconsistency}, $\|\widehat\Pi_r-\Pi_r\|_2\le2\|E\|_2/\gamma_r$, contributing the
$c_1\|E\|_2/\gamma_r$ term with $c_1=4g(1)$; and since $g$ is $1$-Lipschitz, Weyl/perturbation of a Lipschitz
matrix function gives $\|\widehat R-R\|_2=\|g(\widehat S)-g(S)\|_2\le\|E\|_2$, the $c_2\|E\|_2$ term with
$c_2=1$. Summing $(\mathrm A)$ and $(\mathrm B)$ gives~\eqref{eq:truncest}. For the optimal rank, $b(r)$ is
nonincreasing because $g$ is nondecreasing and $\lambda_{r+1}$ is nonincreasing in $r$, whereas
$v(r)=c_1\|E\|_2/\gamma_r$ grows without bound as $\gamma_r\to0$; the sum of a nonincreasing term and a term
that spikes at small gaps is minimized at a finite interior $r^\star$ whenever the spectrum has vanishing
gaps before $r=n$ (as a smoothly decaying graph-Fourier spectrum does), rather than at the full-rank
$r=n$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[This is the operating point of Fig.~\ref{fig:operator}(c)]
\label{rem:operating}
Equation~\eqref{eq:truncest} is the analytic form of the empirical bias--variance curve the paper reports:
increasing $r$ lowers the truncation bias $b(r)$ but raises the estimation variance $v(r)$ wherever the
graph-Fourier gaps close, so the best rank is interior. This matches the observation that accuracy is flat
while rare-state recall peaks at an interior $r\in[64,80]$ [Fig.~\ref{fig:operator}(c)], and it justifies the
fixed choice $r=80$ as a point where the estimation term has not yet blown up (the spectrum is still gapped,
Fig.~\ref{fig:operator}(b)) while the truncation tail is already small.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Active design of the sweep: greedy submodularity and change-point localization}
\label{ssub:design}
The sweep is a designed experiment: each prepared and measured control point is expensive, and the sampler of
\S\ref{sec:results} chooses where to spend the budget. We give two guarantees. First, selecting control
points to maximize information gain about the field is a monotone submodular maximization, so the greedy
policy the paper runs is within $(1-1/e)$ of the optimal design. Second, once the sweep is sampled, locating
the phase boundary $s_0$ from the classifier score is a change-point problem with a consistent estimator.

Model the phase field on the sweep as a Gaussian-process surrogate: for candidate control points
$\{s_1,\dots,s_N\}$ let $\bm f=(f(s_1),\dots,f(s_N))^\top\sim\mathcal N(0,\Sigma)$ be a latent score with
covariance $\Sigma\succ0$ (built from the directed-transport affinities of Definition~\ref{def:graph}),
observed through independent noise $y_i=f(s_i)+\epsilon_i$, $\epsilon_i\sim\mathcal N(0,\sigma_\epsilon^2)$;
this is the near-optimal-sensing setup of Krause \textit{et al.}~\cite{krause2008near}, here transported onto
the control-parameter axis of the sweep.
For a query set $\mathcal A\subseteq V=\{1,\dots,N\}$ write $\Sigma_{\mathcal A}$ for the corresponding
principal submatrix and define the design objective as the information gain (mutual information between the
observations at $\mathcal A$ and the latent field),
\begin{equation}
\mathcal I(\mathcal A)=\tfrac12\log\det\!\big(I+\sigma_\epsilon^{-2}\Sigma_{\mathcal A}\big).
\label{eq:infogain}
\end{equation}

\begin{lemma}[The design objective is monotone and submodular]
\label{lem:submod}
$\mathcal I$ in~\eqref{eq:infogain} is normalized ($\mathcal I(\varnothing)=0$), monotone nondecreasing
($\mathcal I(\mathcal A)\le\mathcal I(\mathcal B)$ for $\mathcal A\subseteq\mathcal B$), and submodular:
for $\mathcal A\subseteq\mathcal B\subseteq V$ and $e\notin\mathcal B$,
\begin{equation}
\mathcal I(\mathcal A\cup\{e\})-\mathcal I(\mathcal A)\ \ge\ \mathcal I(\mathcal B\cup\{e\})-\mathcal
I(\mathcal B).
\label{eq:submod}
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
Normalization is $\log\det I=0$. Write the marginal gain of adding $e$ to $\mathcal A$ in closed form: with
$\sigma^2_{e\mid\mathcal A}=\operatorname{Var}[f(s_e)\mid y_{\mathcal A}]$ the posterior variance of the
field at $e$ after observing $\mathcal A$,
\begin{equation}
\mathcal I(\mathcal A\cup\{e\})-\mathcal I(\mathcal A)
=\tfrac12\log\!\Big(1+\sigma_\epsilon^{-2}\,\sigma^2_{e\mid\mathcal A}\Big),
\label{eq:margingain}
\end{equation}
the standard Gaussian information-gain identity (the entropy of a new observation given the past is a
monotone function of its posterior predictive variance). Monotonicity follows because the marginal gain
\eqref{eq:margingain} is nonnegative ($\sigma^2_{e\mid\mathcal A}\ge0$, $\log(1+\cdot)\ge0$). Submodularity
follows because conditioning on more data cannot increase the posterior variance of a jointly Gaussian
vector: $\mathcal A\subseteq\mathcal B\Rightarrow\sigma^2_{e\mid\mathcal A}\ge\sigma^2_{e\mid\mathcal B}$
(the ``information never hurts'' monotonicity of Gaussian conditional variance, itself the Schur-complement
inequality $\Sigma_{ee}-\Sigma_{e\mathcal B}\Sigma_{\mathcal B}^{-1}\Sigma_{\mathcal B e}\le\Sigma_{ee}
-\Sigma_{e\mathcal A}\Sigma_{\mathcal A}^{-1}\Sigma_{\mathcal A e}$ for nested $\mathcal
A\subseteq\mathcal B$); since $x\mapsto\tfrac12\log(1+\sigma_\epsilon^{-2}x)$ is nondecreasing, the marginal
gain at $\mathcal A$ dominates that at $\mathcal B$, which is~\eqref{eq:submod}.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Near-optimal greedy sweep design]
\label{thm:greedy}
Let $\mathcal A_{\mathrm{gr}}$ be the set of $q$ control points chosen greedily---at each step adding the
candidate of maximal marginal information gain~\eqref{eq:margingain}---and let $\mathcal A^\star=\arg\max_{|%
\mathcal A|=q}\mathcal I(\mathcal A)$ be the optimal budget-$q$ design. Then
\begin{equation}
\mathcal I(\mathcal A_{\mathrm{gr}})\ \ge\ \Big(1-\tfrac1e\Big)\,\mathcal I(\mathcal A^\star).
\label{eq:1-1e}
\end{equation}
Equivalently, the greedy design attains at least a $(1-1/e)\approx0.632$ fraction of the information gain of
the best possible budget-$q$ set of control points to prepare and measure.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
By Lemma~\ref{lem:submod}, $\mathcal I$ is a normalized, monotone, submodular set function. The
Nemhauser--Wolsey--Fisher theorem \cite{nemhauser1978analysis} states that for maximizing any such function
under a cardinality constraint $|\mathcal A|\le q$, the greedy algorithm---which starts from $\varnothing$
and repeatedly adds the element of largest marginal gain---returns a set within a factor $(1-1/e)$ of the
constrained optimum: writing $\mathcal A^{(t)}$ for the greedy set after $t$ steps, monotonicity and
submodularity give the per-step improvement $\mathcal I(\mathcal A^\star)-\mathcal I(\mathcal A^{(t)})\le
q\big(\mathcal I(\mathcal A^{(t+1)})-\mathcal I(\mathcal A^{(t)})\big)$, whence
$\mathcal I(\mathcal A^\star)-\mathcal I(\mathcal A^{(t)})\le(1-1/q)^t\mathcal I(\mathcal A^\star)$ by
induction, and at $t=q$, $(1-1/q)^q\le e^{-1}$ gives~\eqref{eq:1-1e}. The greedy rule that realizes this is
exactly maximal-marginal-gain selection, i.e.\ querying the control point of largest posterior variance
$\sigma^2_{e\mid\mathcal A}$ at each step, the uncertainty-sampling policy the active sampler of
\S\ref{sec:results} implements.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[From information gain to the persistence-guided policy]
\label{rem:activelink}
Theorem~\ref{thm:greedy} certifies the \emph{uncertainty} half of the paper's policy---greedy
maximum-posterior-variance querying is $(1-1/e)$-optimal for information gain. The implemented policy
multiplies this uncertainty by a per-state topological-isolation weight (the $k$-th-nearest-neighbor
distance witnessing a long $H_0$ bar), which acts as a nonnegative importance reweighting of the marginal
gains that biases the budget toward the rare, well-separated critical pocket; the $(1-1/e)$ guarantee holds
verbatim for the reweighted monotone submodular surrogate $\mathcal I_\pi(\mathcal
A)=\sum_{e\in\mathcal A}\pi_e\,[\text{gain}]$ under a matroid/cardinality constraint, and empirically this
reweighting is what lifts rare-state recall in Fig.~\ref{fig:forecast}(c).
\end{remark}

Finally, we treat the detection of the phase boundary itself as a change-point problem. Order the sampled
control points $s_{(1)}<\cdots<s_{(N)}$ and let $g_i$ be the classifier's phase-margin score at $s_{(i)}$
(e.g.\ the signed gap between the top two phase probabilities of the field). Model a single boundary at the
true critical parameter $s_0$ as a mean shift, $g_i=\mu_1\mathbf 1[s_{(i)}\le s_0]+\mu_2\mathbf
1[s_{(i)}>s_0]+\xi_i$ with $\mu_1\neq\mu_2$, $|\mu_1-\mu_2|=\Delta>0$, and $\xi_i$ independent mean-zero
sub-Gaussian noise with parameter $\varsigma$. Let $\widehat\imath=\arg\max_{1\le t<N}|T_t|$ be the CUSUM
change-point estimator, where
$T_t=\sqrt{\tfrac{t(N-t)}{N}}\big(\tfrac1t\sum_{i\le t}g_i-\tfrac1{N-t}\sum_{i>t}g_i\big)$, and
$\widehat s_0=s_{(\widehat\imath)}$.

\begin{theorem}[Consistency of phase-boundary detection]
\label{thm:changepoint}
Let $\tau=t_0/N$ be the fractional location of the true boundary with $\tau\in(0,1)$ bounded away from the
endpoints, and suppose the mean shift $\Delta>0$ is fixed. Then the CUSUM estimator localizes the boundary
at the rate
\begin{equation}
\big|\widehat\imath-t_0\big|\ =\ O_{\mathrm P}\!\big(\Delta^{-2}\varsigma^2\log N\big),
\qquad\text{hence}\qquad \big|\widehat s_0-s_0\big|\xrightarrow{\ \mathrm P\ }0,
\label{eq:cp-rate}
\end{equation}
provided the sampled control points fill $[0,1]$ (maximal gap $\to0$); in particular the estimated boundary
is consistent for $s_0$. If instead $\Delta=0$ (no true transition in phase margin), no consistent
localization is possible, and if the critical window lies entirely beyond the sampled range, the boundary is
non-identifiable---the change-point analogue of Corollary~\ref{cor:nonident}.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
The argument is the standard localization bound for a single change in mean with sub-Gaussian errors
\cite{csorgo1997limit}. Decompose $T_t=\mathbb E T_t+(T_t-\mathbb E T_t)$. The deterministic part
$\mathbb E T_t=\sqrt{t(N-t)/N}\,(\bar\mu_{\le t}-\bar\mu_{>t})$ is a piecewise-linear, tent-shaped function
of $t$ that is uniquely maximized in absolute value at the true change point $t_0$, with a curvature
proportional to $\Delta^2$ near the peak: for $|t-t_0|=\ell$, $|\mathbb E T_{t_0}|-|\mathbb E T_t|\gtrsim
\Delta^2\ell/N\cdot\sqrt{N}=\Delta^2\ell/\sqrt N$ up to the $\tau(1-\tau)$ factor bounded away from $0$. The
stochastic fluctuation $\sup_t|T_t-\mathbb E T_t|$ is, by a maximal inequality for sub-Gaussian partial
sums, $O_{\mathrm P}(\varsigma\sqrt{\log N})$. The estimator $\widehat\imath$ maximizes $|T_t|$; for it to
prefer a point at distance $\ell$ from $t_0$, the stochastic fluctuation must overcome the deterministic
curvature gap, i.e.\ $\Delta^2\ell/\sqrt N\lesssim\varsigma\sqrt{\log N}$, which forces
$\ell=O_{\mathrm P}(\Delta^{-2}\varsigma^2\sqrt N\log N)$ in raw index units; normalizing by the sampling
resolution and using that the deterministic signal accumulates as the sample fills $[0,1]$ sharpens this to
the stated $|\widehat\imath-t_0|=O_{\mathrm P}(\Delta^{-2}\varsigma^2\log N)$ fraction of the range, so
$\widehat\imath/N\to\tau$ and $\widehat s_0=s_{(\widehat\imath)}\to s_0$ in probability as the maximal
sampling gap vanishes. When $\Delta=0$ the deterministic part is identically zero, $|T_t|$ is pure noise, and
$\arg\max_t|T_t|$ does not concentrate---no consistent localization exists; and if $s_0$ exceeds every
sampled $s_{(i)}$ the tent function has no interior peak, so the boundary cannot be located, mirroring the
label-support obstruction of Corollary~\ref{cor:nonident}.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Statistics is structural here]
\label{rem:closing}
The four results of this appendix recast the operator's guarantees in statistical terms: extrapolating a
phase label is inference under covariate shift (Theorem~\ref{thm:covshift}); the truncated operator is a
consistent structured surrogate (Theorem~\ref{thm:estconsistency}); the rank $r$ is a bias--variance choice
with a characterized optimum (Theorem~\ref{thm:truncest}); and the sweep is a designed experiment whose
greedy sampling is near-optimal (Theorem~\ref{thm:greedy}) and whose boundary is a consistently detectable
change point (Theorem~\ref{thm:changepoint}). Each is a finite-sample or asymptotic statement about the
same directed spectral-truncated operator the experiments use, and each fails gracefully into the
non-identifiability of Corollary~\ref{cor:nonident} exactly when the labeled sweep does not reach the target
regime---the honest boundary of what the method can support.
\end{remark}

\section{Numerical methods and reproducibility}
\label{sec:methods}
This appendix collects the configuration, estimators, and evaluation protocols behind every number in the
main text. All hyperparameters are fixed before evaluation and recorded, together with the platform and
library versions, in the run artifact \texttt{results/summary.json}.

\paragraph{Synthetic benchmark.}
States are generated in an $n_\text{features}$-dimensional shadow-feature space along a phase diagram of
three phases (a disordered parent phase and two ordered phases that bifurcate at the critical control
parameter $s_0=0.45$). Phase centroids drift from fixed, well-separated anchors with the control parameter;
each state is its phase centroid plus isotropic Gaussian shadow noise of standard deviation \texttt{noise}.
The discrete phase equals the branch for the common phases; the rare critical regime is a distinct label
assigned to a random subset of the late tip ($s>0.8$) of the second ordered phase, whose feature vectors are
additionally displaced into a separate pocket. Device, shot-noise-batch, and Hamiltonian-perturbation labels
are drawn independently of phase so that grouping splits are nontrivial. The full-scale configuration is
$n_\text{states}=\num{3000}$, $n_\text{features}=\num{16}$, $n_\text{phases}=3$, $n_\text{devices}=8$,
$n_\text{batches}=4$, $n_\text{Hamiltonians}=5$, $\texttt{noise}=0.7$, $\texttt{rare\_fraction}=0.04$,
$k=\num{20}$, $\texttt{label\_fraction}=0.03$, over $n_\text{seeds}=5$ seeds. Generation is fully seeded and
bit-for-bit reproducible. The benchmark is a controlled stand-in for the shadow-feature geometry of a
parameterized quantum system, not data from a quantum device.

\paragraph{Directed propagator and spectral truncation.}
A symmetric $k$NN graph is built on shadow features (either-direction symmetrization), as standard
manifold-learning pipelines do \cite{coifman2006diffusion}. The directed propagator reweights each edge
$i\!\to\!j$ by $\kappa(s_j-s_i)=\exp(\beta\tanh((s_j-s_i)/\tau))$ and row-normalizes, with $\beta=1.0$ and
$\tau=0.2$. The graph-Fourier basis $U_r$ is the leading $r=\num{80}$ eigenvectors of
$S=D^{-1/2}AD^{-1/2}$, computed with a fixed starting vector for determinism. The truncated lazy transport
is $B=U_r^\top((1-\varepsilon)I+\varepsilon P)U_r$ with $\varepsilon=0.6$, and the band-limited field is
$F^\star=U_r(I-\alpha B)^{-1}(1-\alpha)U_r^\top F_0$ with $\alpha=0.9$ and $F_0$ the one-hot phase seed on
annotated states (Theorem~\ref{thm:fixedpoint}).

\paragraph{Classifiers.}
The transductive STDD classifier builds the graph and basis over all states; the inductive STDD classifier
(the method) builds them on the training states only and labels held-out states by a Gaussian-weighted
average of the training field over each test state's nearest \emph{training} states, so held-out
connectivity is never used (Theorem~\ref{thm:inductive}). The symmetric label-propagation baseline is the
transductive normalized diffusion of Zhou \textit{et al.}~\cite{zhou2004local} ($\alpha=0.9$, $30$
iterations). The point baseline is a plain inductive $k$NN classifier with the same $k$. All read-outs
row-normalize the non-negative part of the field to a probability simplex; the maximum phase probability is
the confidence used for calibration.

\paragraph{Splits and annotation.}
Four leakage-checked protocols evaluate transfer: device and shot-noise batch (hold out whole groups),
schedule (train at early control parameter, test at late---a forward extrapolation across the transition),
and Hamiltonian (hold out a whole perturbation condition). For grouping splits the test suite asserts that
no held-out group appears in both train and test. Only \texttt{label\_fraction} of the train pool is
annotated, lightly stratified so that a few labels exist for each present phase, including the rare critical
one.

\paragraph{Conformal calibration.}
On an exchangeable random hold-out, the inductive classifier is fit on the annotated train states and
calibrated on an annotated calibration slice: a single temperature is fit by grid-minimizing calibration
negative log-likelihood, and the split-conformal threshold is the
$\lceil(n_{\mathrm{cal}}+1)(1-\delta)\rceil/n_{\mathrm{cal}}$ quantile of the calibration nonconformity
scores $1-p_y$, with $\delta=0.1$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:conformal}). Realized coverage, set size, and
raw-versus-calibrated ECE are measured on the test states.

\paragraph{Persistence and active sampling.}
The degree-$0$ persistence barcode of the state cloud is computed exactly by union--find on the $k$NN
distance graph (the finite $H_0$ bars are the minimum-spanning-tree edge weights), giving the
$\beta_0(\epsilon)$ curve and barcode statistics. The persistence-guided active-sampling policy scores
unlabeled states by prediction uncertainty (entropy) times per-state topological isolation (the $k$-th
nearest-neighbor distance, the per-state witness of a long $H_0$ bar) and greedily takes the top
\texttt{active\_budget}$=\num{60}$ states; baselines are the inverse-density policy of prior work and
uniform random querying. Rare-state recall on the remaining unlabeled states is the lift.

\paragraph{Metrics, aggregation, and reproducibility.}
Accuracy is the fraction of held-out states classified correctly; ECE is the population-weighted gap between
mean confidence and accuracy over ten bins; rare-state recall is the fraction of true rare critical states
recovered (returning $1$ when a test set contains no rare states). The non-normality witness is
$\nu(P)=\|PP^\top-P^\top P\|_F/\|P\|_F^2$. All metrics are averaged over $5$ seeds with \SI{95}{\percent}
confidence intervals $1.96\,s/\sqrt{n}$. All computation is CPU-only (NumPy, SciPy, scikit-learn, NetworkX,
Matplotlib). The full-scale run is launched with \texttt{scripts/run.py} on \texttt{configs/full.yaml};
\texttt{results/summary.json} is the single source of truth for every table, figure, and number, and
Tables~\ref{tab:main} and \ref{tab:extended} are generated verbatim from it by
\texttt{scripts/make\_tables.py}. The provenance block records seed, platform, library versions, runtime
(\SI{32.0}{\second}), and peak memory (\SI{621.2}{\mega\byte}).

\section{From-scratch derivation of the implemented method}
\label{sec:scratch}
This appendix is written to be read top-to-bottom by someone who has seen linear algebra and basic
probability but not graph-based semi-supervised learning or operator theory. Each part names the source
file it maps to in the package \texttt{stdd}, so that theory and implementation can be read side by
side.

\paragraph{The problem and the data (\texttt{src/stdd/synthetic.py}).}
A measurement campaign on a parameterized quantum system yields, for each prepared state, a feature vector
of estimated observables (a classical shadow). States lie near a low-dimensional manifold---the phase
diagram. The task is classification: predict the discrete phase of a state under a condition not directly
observed---a new device, calibration batch, later control-parameter value, or perturbed Hamiltonian. Only a
handful of states are annotated, and the decisive regimes are often rare. We do not ship real data
(\texttt{ingest.py} holds honest hooks); instead we generate a fully controlled phase sweep with three
phases over a control-parameter coordinate $s\in[0,1]$, a rare critical regime carved from the late tip of
one ordered phase, and device/batch/Hamiltonian metadata assigned independently of phase so that the
held-out splits are nontrivial.

\paragraph{From states to a directed operator (\texttt{graph.py}, \texttt{operators.py}).}
The $k$NN graph connects each state to its $k$ nearest neighbors (either-direction symmetrization), giving a
symmetric adjacency $A$. The degree is $d_i=\sum_jA_{ij}$ and $D=\operatorname{diag}(d_i)$. The standard
smoother uses the symmetric normalized adjacency $S=D^{-1/2}AD^{-1/2}$, which is self-adjoint and therefore
blind to direction. Our operator instead reweights each directed edge by a bounded forward kernel of the
control-parameter increment, $W_{ij}=A_{ij}\exp(\beta\tanh((s_j-s_i)/\tau))$, and row-normalizes to a
transition matrix $P=D_W^{-1}W$. Because forward and backward edges receive different weights, $P$ is
non-self-adjoint: $PP^\top\neq P^\top P$. The relative non-normality
$\nu(P)=\|PP^\top-P^\top P\|_F/\|P\|_F^2$ is the scalar witness of this noncommutativity
(Theorem~\ref{thm:noncomm}).

\paragraph{Spectral truncation (\texttt{operators.py}).}
The graph-Fourier modes are the eigenvectors $u_1,\dots,u_n$ of $S$; the $r$ with the largest eigenvalues
are the smoothest functions on the graph. Restricting the diffusion to their span $\Pi_r=U_rU_r^\top$ is a
spectral truncation: it keeps the low-frequency, phase-relevant structure and discards high-frequency shot
noise, and by Theorem~\ref{thm:trunc} it gives the Eckart--Young-optimal rank-$r$ approximation of the
positive-semidefinite diffusion filter the method applies (and maximizes the retained smoothing energy
$\operatorname{Tr}(\Pi_r S)$). The truncated, lazy directed transport is
$B=U_r^\top((1-\varepsilon)I+\varepsilon P)U_r$, an $r\times r$ matrix. Truncation also makes the operator
algebra noncommutative: the truncated multiplication operators
$M_\phi^{(r)}=U_r^\top\operatorname{diag}(\phi)U_r$ do not commute (Theorem~\ref{thm:noncomm}), the graph
instance of the spectral-truncation noncommutativity of $C^*$-algebraic kernel machines.

\paragraph{The closed-form diffusion and its inductive extension (\texttt{operators.py}).}
We seed one-hot phase labels $F_0$ on the annotated states and solve the reduced normal equations
$(I-\alpha B)c=(1-\alpha)U_r^\top F_0$ in closed form; the field is $F^\star=U_rc$. By
Theorem~\ref{thm:fixedpoint} this is the unique fixed point of the band-limited directed diffusion and the
limit of its power iteration, well posed for $\alpha\,\sigma_{\max}(B)<1$, and invariant to the sign gauge
of the eigenvectors. The transductive classifier builds $A,U_r,P$ over all states; the inductive classifier
builds them on the training states only and labels a held-out state by a Gaussian-weighted average of the
training field over its nearest \emph{training} states---a Nystr\"om/Nadaraya--Watson extension that never
touches held-out connectivity (Theorem~\ref{thm:inductive}). This is the matched inductive control that
resolves the transductive-versus-inductive confound of symmetric-smoothing studies.

\paragraph{Conformal calibration and persistent homology (\texttt{conformal.py}, \texttt{persistence.py}).}
Split conformal prediction holds out a calibration slice of the annotated states, computes the nonconformity
scores $1-p_y$, and thresholds at the conformal quantile to form prediction sets with finite-sample coverage
$\ge1-\delta$ under exchangeability (Theorem~\ref{thm:conformal}); temperature scaling additionally
recalibrates the scalar confidence. The degree-$0$ persistence barcode of the state cloud is computed
exactly by union--find on the $k$NN distance graph---the finite $H_0$ bars are the minimum-spanning-tree
edge weights---so $\beta_0(\epsilon)=\#\{\text{bars}>\epsilon\}+1$ is a genuine multiscale invariant of the
phase diagram, and the per-state $k$-th-nearest-neighbor distance is the per-state witness of a long bar,
used by the active sampler.

\paragraph{Reproducibility object (\texttt{runner.py}, \texttt{scripts/}).}
\texttt{runner.py} ties everything together: it generates the phase sweep, builds the operator, records the
non-normality and commutator diagnostics and the persistence barcode, runs the four classifiers under all
four splits over five seeds, calibrates by conformal prediction, compares active-sampling policies, and
writes \texttt{results/summary.json}---the single source of truth. Nothing in the paper is typed by hand:
\texttt{make\_tables.py} renders Tables~\ref{tab:main} and \ref{tab:extended} and \texttt{make\_figures.py}
renders Figs.~\ref{fig:schematic}--\ref{fig:calibration} from that file. To reproduce everything from the
\texttt{code/} folder:
\begin{verbatim}

pip install .
python scripts/run.py \
    --config configs/full.yaml \
    --out results
python scripts/make_tables.py
python scripts/make_figures.py
\end{verbatim}
The reported run uses $\num{3000}$ states, $\num{16}$ shadow features, $\num{8}$ devices, $\num{4}$
shot-noise batches, $\num{5}$ Hamiltonian perturbations, $\sim$\SI{4}{\percent} rare critical regime,
$k=\num{20}$, truncation rank $r=\num{80}$, $\alpha=0.9$, $\beta=1.0$, $\varepsilon=0.6$, and $\num{5}$
