Directed spectral-truncated operators for inductive classification of quantum phases across a control-parameter sweep

Read from the keyboard, a quantum simulator is a stochastic device: every preparation returns a single measurement record, so identifying the phase it occupies is statistical inference from finite, noisy samples and demands calibrated uncertainty. The graph smoother used almost universally for such data is self-adjoint, blind to the arrow of the drive, and, built over all states, transductive. This work replaces it with a drive-directed, spectrally truncated, inductive graph operator: a non-self-adjoint (noncommutative) propagator transports phase labels forward along the sweep, spectral truncation band-limits and denoises it into a closed-form solve, and an out-of-sample Nyström extension removes the transductive confound. On a fully seeded synthetic benchmark the operator improves held-out accuracy over a matched point classifier on all four transfer protocols, recovers a rare critical regime the baseline almost never finds, rescues forward extrapolation across the transition, and is conformally calibrated with distribution-free coverage. Every figure, table and number is generated by a pip-installable package.

One operator-algebraic program

This work is one entry in a program that treats the spectral truncation of operators as a common design primitive across quantum optimization, simulation, and error correction. Here that primitive is carried onto the state graph of a parameterized quantum many-body system, where a band-limiting projector plays the role the residual or schedule truncation plays elsewhere in the series, and realizes the spectral truncation of multiplication operators that a companion operator-spectral prior uses for schedule transfer.

1Background and Problem Setting

A quantum simulator is, from the keyboard, a stochastic device interrogated only by sampling. The Born rule means each run returns a single bitstring drawn from a distribution, so recognizing the phase a prepared state occupies is not a deterministic readout but an inference problem — estimating a discrete label from a finite, noisy sample and attaching honest uncertainty to it.

Programmable quantum simulators prepare and measure many-body states across entire phase diagrams. The standard feature representation is the classical shadow: a randomized-measurement statistic that compresses an exponentially large state into a tractable, low-dimensional vector. Across a control-parameter sweep, these shadow features concentrate near a low-dimensional manifold — the phase diagram — routinely approximated by a k-nearest-neighbour (kNN) similarity graph. The canonical estimator on such a graph is graph-based semi-supervised learning: phase labels are diffused over the state graph so that each state borrows statistical strength from its neighbourhood. The diffusion operator used almost universally is the symmetric normalized adjacency $S=D^{-1/2}AD^{-1/2}$. Two properties of this object limit it for classification across a sweep.

It is self-adjoint — blind to direction

$S$ is symmetric, hence reversible: it cannot encode the directed arrow of the drive, even though the control-parameter sweep supplies exactly that arrow and directed, non-Hermitian transition operators are the native language of driven and open quantum dynamics. Classifying forward across the transition is precisely where a symmetric smoother is weakest.

Transductively evaluated, it is confounded

Built over all states, the propagation graph consults the held-out states’ connectivity at inference. A measured accuracy gain over an inductive point classifier therefore conflates the value of diffusion with a transductive information advantage — the matched inductive control that symmetric-smoothing studies flag as their decisive missing experiment.

We take both properties as design targets and build a different object: a drive-directed, spectrally truncated, inductive graph operator. A second difficulty compounds them — the physically decisive regimes, quantum critical points and narrow topological windows, are rare, and classifiers trained to minimize average error miss them. Recovering such a regime with calibrated uncertainty is the open problem the operator is designed to solve.

All experiments run on a seeded synthetic benchmark that emulates the shadow-feature geometry of a parameterized quantum system. This is a controlled, falsifiable stand-in, not quantum-device data; that scope is stated explicitly throughout and bounds every claim below.

2The Operator

Fix $n$ states embedded in a shadow-feature space $\mathbb{R}^{d}$ with a symmetric kNN adjacency $A$, degrees $D=\operatorname{diag}(d_i)$ and a control-parameter coordinate $s\in\mathbb{R}^n$. The classifier is a single operator built from three coupled constructions, each tied to a measured effect.

Drive-directed propagator (noncommutativity)

Each directed edge $i\!\to\! j$ is reweighted by a bounded forward kernel of the control-parameter increment and row-normalized, $$ W_{ij}=A_{ij}\,\exp\!\bigl(\beta\tanh\tfrac{s_j-s_i}{\tau}\bigr),\qquad P=D_W^{-1}W, $$ giving a row-stochastic but non-self-adjoint propagator that transports labels forward along the sweep. Its relative non-normality $\nu(P)=\|PP^{\top}-P^{\top}P\|_F/\|P\|_F^2$ is strictly positive and increases with the directional strength $\beta$; this is the state-graph instance of the operator noncommutativity exploited by $C^{*}$-algebraic kernel machines.

Spectral truncation (band-limiting)

Let $U_r$ collect the leading $r$ graph-Fourier modes (the eigenvectors of $S$ for its largest eigenvalues). Restricting the diffusion to their span $\Pi_r=U_rU_r^{\top}$ is a spectral truncation: for the positive-semidefinite diffusion filter the method applies, it is the Eckart–Young–Mirsky-optimal rank-$r$ low-pass approximation, and the truncated, lazy directed transport $$ B=U_r^{\top}\bigl((1-\varepsilon)I+\varepsilon P\bigr)U_r $$ is an $r\times r$ matrix admitting a closed-form solve. Truncation also makes the operator algebra noncommutative: the truncated multiplication operators $M_\phi^{(r)}=U_r^{\top}\operatorname{diag}(\phi)U_r$ do not commute — the spectral-truncation noncommutativity of noncommutative geometry, here on the state graph — and they generate a positive-definite Gram kernel $\mathcal K=\sum_c M_cM_c^{\top}$.

Inductive Nyström extension (no transductive confound)

The band-limited field is solved on the training states, $F^{\star}=U_r(I-\alpha B)^{-1}(1-\alpha)U_r^{\top}F_0$, and a held-out state is labelled by a Gaussian-weighted average of that field over its nearest training states. No test–test edge and no held-out connectivity is ever used at inference, so the classifier is genuinely inductive — the matched inductive control that resolves the transductive confound of symmetric smoothing, and the property that lets the trained operator transfer to states from a device it never saw.

A split-conformal layer calibrates the rare-regime prediction with distribution-free coverage, and the degree-0 persistence barcode of the state cloud replaces the passive connected-component count of prior work with an informative multiscale topology signal that guides active sampling.

3Principal Contributions

  1. A drive-directed, noncommutative graph propagator. Reweighting kNN edges by a bounded forward kernel of the control-parameter increment yields a non-self-adjoint operator whose relative non-normality is strictly positive ($\nu(P)=0.0370$) and grows with the directional strength; the drive contributes ~36% of it over the undirected control ($\nu(P_0)=0.0272$). The directed transport carries labels along the sweep, which a symmetric smoother cannot do.
  2. Spectral truncation with proved optimality. Band-limiting the diffusion to the leading $r$ graph-Fourier modes is the optimal rank-$r$ low-pass approximation of the monotone positive-semidefinite diffusion filter (Eckart–Young–Mirsky), and the truncated multiplication operators on the state graph do not commute — the spectral-truncation noncommutativity of noncommutative geometry, with a positive-definite Gram kernel ($\lambda_{\min}=1.62>0$).
  3. An inductive operator that removes the transductive confound. An out-of-sample Nyström extension labels held-out states from the training field alone; the operator beats a matched inductive kNN classifier on accuracy on all four transfer protocols (mean $0.6856$ vs $0.5992$, gain $0.0864$), with the gain not attributable to a transductive information advantage.
  4. Rare-regime recovery and forward-extrapolation rescue. The operator lifts mean rare-state recall to $0.3322$ from a near-zero baseline ($0.0151$), and the directional transport raises forward-extrapolation (drive-schedule) accuracy by $0.1749$ over the point classifier and by $0.0850$ over symmetric label propagation.
  5. Distribution-free conformal calibration. A split-conformal layer attains realized marginal coverage $0.8968$ against a target of $0.90$ on an exchangeable hold-out, and temperature scaling cuts expected calibration error from $0.1374$ to $0.0369$ — turning the calibration penalty of graph smoothing into a calibration guarantee.
  6. A genuine multiscale topology signal. The degree-0 persistence barcode (computed exactly by union–find) replaces a constant connected-component count; a persistence-guided active sampler raises rare-state recall to $0.3941$ against $0.0481$ for random querying.
  7. Operator-theoretic guarantees and deterministic reproducibility. Theorems certify truncation optimality, noncommutativity, well-posedness, inductive consistency and conformal coverage; a pip-installable package (specops-dsto) regenerates every table, figure and number from a single seeded summary.json.

4Theoretical Guarantees

The statements below are transcribed from the manuscript’s self-contained theory section, each annotated with a one-line reading. Together they establish that the operator is well posed, genuinely noncommutative, optimally band-limited, inductively consistent and conformally valid.

Theorem 1 (Spectral truncation is the optimal band-limit).

Among all rank-$r$ orthogonal projectors, the band-limiting projector $\Pi_r=U_rU_r^{\top}$ maximizes the retained smoothing energy $\operatorname{Tr}(\Pi S)$; and for any positive-semidefinite graph low-pass filter $R=g(S)$ with $g$ nondecreasing — in particular the diffusion resolvent and heat kernel the method realizes — $\Pi_r$ furnishes the Eckart–Young–Mirsky-optimal rank-$r$ approximation of $R$ in every unitarily invariant norm.

The leading graph-Fourier modes are the provably best rank-$r$ low-pass approximation of the smoother the classifier applies — spectral truncation is principled, not heuristic.
Theorem 2 (Noncommutativity of the directed propagator and of truncation).

The directed propagator is non-normal, $[P,P^{\top}]\ne 0$, whenever the outgoing and incoming squared transition mass differ at some state; and the truncated multiplication operators satisfy $[M_\phi^{(r)},M_\psi^{(r)}]\ne 0$ in general, vanishing only in the full-rank limit $\Pi_r=I$.

Both the directionality and the truncation are genuinely noncommutative — the state-graph instance of the spectral-truncation noncommutativity of $C^{*}$-algebraic kernel machines.
Theorem 3 (Well-posedness and closed form of the truncated directed diffusion).

If $\rho(\alpha B)<1$ then $I-\alpha B$ is invertible, the reduced field $F^{\star}=U_r(I-\alpha B)^{-1}(1-\alpha)U_r^{\top}F_0$ is the unique fixed point of the band-limited power iteration with geometric convergence, and it is invariant to the eigenvector sign gauge. At the fixed rank $r=80$, $\rho(\alpha B)=0.9209<1$.

The closed-form solve the code runs is well posed and gauge-independent — a faithful, geometrically convergent realization of the operator.
Theorem 4 (Inductive consistency of the Nyström extension).

The out-of-sample estimator uses no test–test edge and no held-out connectivity, so the classifier is inductive; and for an $L$-Lipschitz field, $\|\widehat F(x_\ast)-F^{\star}(x_\ast)\|_2\le L\max_{i}\|x_i-x_\ast\|_2$, which vanishes as the training sample becomes dense.

The inductive extension is a consistent estimator of the band-limited field — the matched inductive control, made rigorous, that removes the transductive confound.
Theorem 5 (Distribution-free conformal coverage).

For nonconformity score $s=1-p_y$ and the conformal quantile $\hat q$ of $n_{\text{cal}}$ calibration scores exchangeable with the test state, the prediction set $C(x)=\{c:1-p_c(x)\le\hat q\}$ satisfies $\Pr[y_{\text{test}}\in C(x_{\text{test}})]\ge 1-\delta$.

The rare-regime prediction carries a finite-sample, distribution-free coverage guarantee under exchangeability — the calibration penalty of smoothing becomes a calibration guarantee.
Corollary (Non-identifiability of a label-free region).

A class absent from the labelled seed on every state reachable from the test region receives zero diffused mass there and cannot be recovered — the structural mechanism behind the null schedule-split rare-state recall, where the rare critical states lie beyond the labelled range of the control parameter.

Smoothing cannot invent an unreachable class; the schedule-split null is reported as-is, and the theory explains exactly why.

Reading of the accuracy result

The inductive operator improves accuracy on every transfer protocol, not on average only, and the comparison is inductive on both sides — so the gain is not the transductive information advantage that inflates symmetric-smoothing studies. It even exceeds transductive symmetric label propagation ($0.6587$) despite operating in the harder inductive regime. The largest margin is on the forward drive-schedule split, where the directed transport is decisive.

Reading of the rare-regime result

The operator recovers a rare critical regime the point classifier almost never finds, at $22\times$ its mean recall. The drive-schedule null is reported as-is: forward extrapolation places the rare critical states beyond the labelled range of the control parameter, a label-free region the corollary proves diffusion cannot reach. Wide intervals on per-split rare recall follow from small absolute rare counts.

7Significance and Impact

Within a controlled benchmark, a drive-directed, spectrally truncated, inductive graph operator improves quantum-phase classification where the standard symmetric smoother is weakest — recovering a rare critical regime the point classifier almost never finds, and rescuing forward extrapolation across the transition — while removing the transductive confound and supplying distribution-free calibration.

For the machine-learning-for-quantum-physics program, the result is a directional, calibrated classifier that converts the arrow of the control-parameter sweep into rare-regime recovery, with a persistence-guided annotation policy that targets the sparse manifold pockets where critical states reside — each query being an expensive state preparation and measurement. For machine learning under covariate shift more broadly, the operator carries the spectral-truncation and noncommutativity machinery of $C^{*}$-algebraic kernel machines onto a graph-diffusion classifier and pairs it with an inductive extension that makes the diffusion gain attributable to one mechanism rather than to a confounded design choice. The accompanying theory certifies that the deployed solve faithfully realizes a well-posed, gauge-invariant estimator with a finite-sample coverage guarantee.

The decisive next step is to repeat exactly this comparison on classical shadows from a programmable quantum simulator and on perturbed-Hamiltonian families, for which the repository provides intentionally unexercised ingest stubs. The operator extends naturally to noisier, higher-dimensional drives, to higher-order persistence, and to a learned directed propagator in place of the fixed-kernel transport.

Limitations (stated in full)

(1) Synthetic data. All numbers come from a generated benchmark that emulates shadow-feature geometry, not measurements from a quantum device; the magnitudes need not transfer to real classical-shadow data, where shot noise, finite sampling, and device drift are far more severe.
(2) The transductive operator trades some accuracy. Held fixed in the transductive setting the directed operator nearly doubles symmetric label propagation’s rare-state recall but loses a few points of mean accuracy; it is the inductive variant that recovers and exceeds the baseline’s accuracy while removing the confound.
(3) Calibration is repaired post hoc, under exchangeability. The conformal guarantee holds for exchangeable calibration and test states; under grouping-transfer distribution shift (a genuinely new device or Hamiltonian), realized coverage is only approximate.
(4) Directionality requires a control-parameter estimate. The operator consumes a control-parameter coordinate; on real data this is the experimentally set drive, but an unparameterized or mislabelled sweep would weaken or mislead the directional transport.
(5) Standard degree-0 topology. The persistence barcode is a genuine multiscale invariant but $H_0$ only; the truncation-commutator noncommutativity and the Gram kernel are finite-rank witnesses, not a full $C^{*}$-algebraic construction.
(6) Single benchmark family. One synthetic generator, however controlled, cannot substitute for the diversity of real quantum systems; external validation remains the decisive test.

8Reproducibility

The reference implementation is the pip-installable package specops-dsto at submission/code (CPU-only: NumPy, SciPy, scikit-learn, NetworkX, Matplotlib). A single seeded results/summary.json is the authoritative record for every table, figure and number; the manuscript’s tables are generated verbatim from it, and no value here is hand-entered.

# 1. install the CPU reference package
cd submission/code
pip install specops-dsto       # or, from a checkout: pip install .

# 2. reproduce every number, table and figure
pytest tests/                                  # operator / conformal / persistence property tests
python scripts/run.py --config configs/full.yaml --out results   # -> results/summary.json (~32 s, 621 MB)
python scripts/make_tables.py                  # results/main_results.tex, extended_results.tex
python scripts/make_figures.py                 # figures/fig_{schematic,sweep,forecast,operator,calibration}.pdf
python scripts/audit_claims.py                 # readiness gate: traceable numbers, integrity flags

# or use the installed entry point end-to-end
dsto-reproduce --config configs/full.yaml      # run + tables + figures

# 3. build the manuscript
cd .. && tectonic main.tex                      # or: latexmk -pdf main.tex

Three integrity gates must pass before any metric is recorded: the grouping splits are leakage-free, the directed propagator is non-normal, and the rare regime is present. A failed gate halts the run rather than emitting a number.

Recorded provenance (deterministic results)

Master seed $0$ · runtime 32.0 s · peak memory 621.2 MB. Generation is fully seeded and bit-for-bit reproducible (a fixed ARPACK starting vector makes the spectral truncation deterministic). Ingest stubs for real classical-shadow and perturbed-Hamiltonian data are included but intentionally not exercised.

No quantum-device data were used or generated. All results derive from a seeded synthetic benchmark regenerated deterministically by the code.

9References and Related Work

The construction sits at the intersection of several literatures. Its diffusion backbone is graph-based semi-supervised learning — label propagation and learning with local and global consistency (Zhu & Ghahramani; Zhou et al.). Its task is the recognition of quantum phases from measurement data — a workhorse of machine learning for quantum physics, typically over classical-shadow / randomized-measurement features (Carrasquilla & Melko; Huang et al.; Carleo et al.). Its arrow comes from directed, non-Hermitian transition operators native to driven and open quantum dynamics, and from spectral learning on directed graphs (Chung; Zhang et al.). It is made inductive by an out-of-sample Nyström extension (Williams & Seeger; Bengio et al.), calibrated by distribution-free conformal prediction (Vovk et al.; Lei et al.), and read out with a persistent-homology descriptor (Edelsbrunner & Harer; Carlsson). The non-self-adjoint propagator and the truncated multiplication operators are the state-graph instance of the operator noncommutativity that recent $C^{*}$-algebraic spectral-truncation kernel machines exploit for expressiveness (Hashimoto et al.; van Suijlekom; Connes & van Suijlekom).

This work is one entry in a broader program that treats the spectral truncation of operators as a common design primitive across quantum optimization, simulation, and error correction; the full citation set, including that series, appears in the manuscript.
